\begin{abstract}
Continuous outcomes such as quality-of-life scores are often undefined after death or a related event. A common pragmatic solution is to encode such patients by the worst possible score and analyze the resulting composite outcome with a one-dimensional procedure. That preserves a clinically meaningful ordering, but it conflates treatment effects on the atom with treatment effects on the continuous part among those with observed outcomes. We propose likelihood-ratio tests for the joint null of no treatment effect on either component. The construction yields a single prespecified p-value together with interpretable component-wise effect estimates and confidence regions. We present both a parametric version and a semi-parametric version based on empirical likelihood, establish their large-sample calibration, and study their behavior in simulations. The methods are most advantageous when component effects can be obscured by the one-dimensional composite or when survivor-distribution features make standard combined-outcome tests inefficient. A clinical application to the IST-3 acute ischemic stroke trial illustrates the method using death by six months as the atom and EuroQol visual analogue score among survivors as the continuous component.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Continuous outcomes such as quality-of-life scores, cognition scores, and days alive outside hospital are increasingly used in randomized trials of serious illness. When patients die before assessment, however, those outcomes are undefined rather than merely missing, creating the truncation-by-death problem \citep{Frangakis_2002,Colantuoni_2018}. Composite outcomes that combine mortality with an otherwise continuous scale are common in critical care and related areas; familiar examples include ventilator-free days and days alive without life support \citep{10.1001/jama.2021.18295,Yehya_2019}.

One pragmatic response is to place death, or another substantively distinct event, at the bottom of a single ordered scale and analyze the resulting composite with a rank-based or related one-dimensional method. Worst-rank analyses and related composite rankings have been advocated precisely because they retain all randomized patients in the primary analysis \citep{Lachin_1999,McMahon_2000,Colantuoni_2018}. The same logic underlies the widespread use of atom-at-zero outcomes such as ventilator-free days, where death is assigned the worst possible value on the scale \citep{Yehya_2019}. Throughout, we refer to the Mann--Whitney--Wilcoxon rank-sum test simply as the Wilcoxon test \citep{wilcoxon3001968individual,mann1947test}.

That strategy is often clinically sensible, but it targets the distribution of the composite outcome itself. It does not separate treatment effects on the probability of the atom from treatment effects on the continuous outcome among patients with observed values. When the two components move in different directions, or when treatment acts mainly through one of them, a one-dimensional analysis can be attenuated because opposite component effects partially cancel on the combined scale. The issue is therefore not only one of robustness or convenience; it is also one of estimand alignment.

Other approaches address truncated-by-death outcomes from different perspectives. Survivor-only analyses condition on a post-randomization event and therefore answer a conditional question that can differ materially from the intention-to-treat comparison. Principal-stratification and survivor-average-causal-effect approaches instead target causal effects in latent survivor strata, but they require assumptions that are not identified from the observed trial data alone \citep{Frangakis_2002,Colantuoni_2018}. Composite-endpoint approaches avoid that latent-stratum target by working directly with an ordered endpoint, but then the estimand is tied to the chosen ranking \citep{McMahon_2000,Colantuoni_2018}. Our goal in this paper is narrower and more operational: we target the joint observed-data null of no treatment effect on the atom probability and no treatment effect on the continuous component among those for whom it is observed.

Our construction combines ideas from two-part models for semi-continuous outcomes \citep{aitchison1955distribution,cragg1971some} and empirical-likelihood inference for mean restrictions \citep{owen1988empirical,owen1991empirical,kitamura2001asymptotic}. We develop a factorized likelihood-ratio framework that yields a single prespecified p-value for the joint null together with interpretable component-wise effect estimates and confidence regions. We present both a parametric version, which is efficient under a credible model for the positive part, and a semi-parametric version, which relaxes that distributional commitment while retaining the same Bernoulli component. The resulting procedures preserve the clinically meaningful atom used in practice, but they test a sharper question than direct one-dimensional analyses of the combined outcome.

Although we motivate the method by quality of life truncated by death, the same structure arises whenever one special value has a substantively different meaning than the remainder of an otherwise continuous scale. The target is an observed-data treatment contrast, not a principal-stratum causal estimand. The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:method} introduces the statistical setup and the proposed tests. Section~\ref{sec:simulation-study} reports a simulation study, Section~\ref{sec:application} gives a clinical application, and Section~\ref{sec:discussion} concludes. Secondary package-data applications, additional simulation results, and a derivation of the empirical-likelihood component are collected in Appendix~\ref{app:joiner-liver-application}, Appendix~\ref{app:licorice-gargle-application}, Appendix~\ref{app:supplementary-simulation-results}, and Appendix~\ref{app:supplementary-derivation}.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Setup and scientific null}

We observe independent and identically distributed random variables $(Y_i, A_i, R_i, X_i)$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n$. Here $Y_i$ is a continuous outcome when it is defined, $A_i \in \{0,1\}$ indicates whether the outcome is defined and observed, $R_i \in \{0,1\}$ is the treatment indicator, and $X_i$ is a $p$-dimensional vector of baseline covariates. Because $Y_i$ is only meaningful when $A_i = 1$, the scientific null hypothesis of no treatment effect is
\begin{align*}
  \mathcal{L}(Y_i \mid A_i = 1, R_i = 1, X_i)
  &= \mathcal{L}(Y_i \mid A_i = 1, R_i = 0, X_i),\\
  \Pr(A_i = 1 \mid R_i = 1, X_i)
  &= \Pr(A_i = 1 \mid R_i = 0, X_i).
\end{align*}
In practice, analysts often replace this bivariate object by a single combined outcome. Let $\mathcal{Y} \subset \mathbb{R}$ be a compact set containing the support of $Y_i \mid A_i = 1$, and let $\mathcal{E} \notin \mathcal{Y}$ be a distinguished symbol representing the undefined outcome. We define the outcome space by
\[
  \widetilde{\mathcal{Y}} = \mathcal{Y} \sqcup \{\mathcal{E}\},
\]
where $\sqcup$ denotes disjoint union. We equip $\widetilde{\mathcal{Y}}$ with the $\sigma$-algebra
\[
  \widetilde{\mathcal{B}} = \{B,\; B \cup \{\mathcal{E}\} : B \in \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{Y})\},
\]
where $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{Y})$ is the Borel $\sigma$-algebra on $\mathcal{Y}$ induced by $\mathbb{R}$. Thus $\mathcal{E}$ is an abstract atom, distinct from every point of $\mathcal{Y}$, and $\widetilde{Y}_i$ is a $\widetilde{\mathcal{Y}}$-valued random variable. For fixed $e_0 \in \mathbb{R}$, we define the numerical representation map
\[
  c_{e_0}:\widetilde{\mathcal{Y}} \to \mathbb{R}, \qquad c_{e_0}(y)=y \text{ for } y \in \mathcal{Y}, \qquad c_{e_0}(\mathcal{E}) = e_0,
\]
and arithmetic is performed on $c_{e_0}(\widetilde{Y}_i)$, not on the abstract atom itself. When no ambiguity arises, one may equivalently work directly on the numerical outcome space $\mathcal{Y} \cup \{e_0\} \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, thereby identifying the atom with the actual number $e_0$. Throughout most of the manuscript we use the atom-at-zero representation $c_0$, obtained by setting $e_0 = 0$.
\begin{align}
  \widetilde{Y}_i
  &=
  \begin{cases}
    Y_i, & A_i = 1,\\
    \mathcal{E}, & A_i = 0.
  \end{cases}\label{eq:combinedOutcome}
\end{align}
where $\mathcal{E}$ is a fixed atom representing the undefined outcome. The distribution of $\widetilde{Y}_i$ is therefore a mixture of a point mass at $\mathcal{E}$ and a continuous distribution on the support of $Y_i \mid A_i = 1$.
\[
  \widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0} := c_0(\widetilde{Y}_i)
  =
  \begin{cases}
    Y_i, & A_i = 1,\\
    0, & A_i = 0.
  \end{cases}
\]
This is the real-valued combined outcome used whenever expectations or other arithmetic summaries are taken.

The conditional mean of the numerical combined outcome is
\begin{align*}
  \E[\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0} \mid R_i, X_i]
  = \E[Y_i \mid A_i = 1, R_i, X_i]\Pr(A_i = 1 \mid R_i, X_i).
\end{align*}
This quantity can be identical between treatment groups even when treatment affects both the observation probability and the continuous component. To see this, let
\[
\E[Y_i \mid A_i = 1, R_i = 0, X_i] = \mu(X_i), \qquad
\E[Y_i \mid A_i = 1, R_i = 1, X_i] = \mu(X_i) + \mu_\delta(X_i),
\]
Then
\begin{align}
  \Delta_{\widetilde{Y}}(X_i)
  &= \E[\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0} \mid R_i = 1, X_i] - \E[\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0} \mid R_i = 0, X_i]\label{eq:Delta}\\
  &= \mu(X_i)\left[\Pr(A_i = 1 \mid R_i = 1, X_i) - \Pr(A_i = 1 \mid R_i = 0, X_i)\right]\nonumber\\
  &\quad + \mu_\delta(X_i)\Pr(A_i = 1 \mid R_i = 1, X_i).\nonumber
\end{align}
Here $\Delta_{\widetilde{Y}}(X_i)$ is a contrast on the atom-at-zero numerical representation $\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0}$, not a mean on the abstract disjoint-union space.
Choosing
\[
\mu_\delta(X_i)
= \mu(X_i)\left[\Pr(A_i = 1 \mid R_i = 0, X_i)\Pr(A_i = 1 \mid R_i = 1, X_i)^{-1} - 1\right]
\]
forces $\Delta_{\widetilde{Y}}(X_i)=0$ even though both components depend on treatment. Thus testing $\Delta_{\widetilde{Y}}(X_i)=0$ is generally not equivalent to testing no treatment effect on the joint observed-data structure, and the latter requires two restrictions.

In the abstract formulation, $\mathcal{E}$ is a fixed prespecified atom representing the clinically worst state on the combined scale. When numerical summaries are required, we pass to a real-valued representation $c_{e_0}$; throughout most of the manuscript, the default choice is $e_0 = 0$. In applications where the observed endpoint truly has an atom at a real number such as $0$, we may work directly on that numerical scale. The event $A_i = 0$ therefore denotes the substantive atom event, such as death before assessment or zero days alive out of hospital, rather than ordinary measurement missingness. Our estimands are defined with respect to the observed-data law of $(A_i, Y_i \mid A_i = 1)$, so identifiability is with respect to that observed-data structure rather than a latent principal stratum. The inferential development below assumes positive probabilities of $A_i = 1$ in both treatment groups, identifiable finite-dimensional models for the Bernoulli component, and, when the parametric procedure is used, a correctly specified model for the conditional outcome distribution.

To exploit that structure, we model the binary and continuous components separately. Let
\begin{align*}
  A_i \mid R_i, X_i &\sim \Bernoulli\left(\pi_i\right),\\
  Y_i \mid A_i = 1, R_i, X_i &\sim F\left(\cdot; \mu_i, \Psi\right),
\end{align*}
where $\pi_i = \Pr(A_i = 1 \mid R_i, X_i)$ is the observation probability, $\mu_i = \E[Y_i \mid A_i = 1, R_i, X_i]$ is a mean parameter for the observed outcomes, and $\Psi$ collects nuisance parameters governing the distribution of the continuous component. If $f(\cdot; \mu_i, \Psi)$ denotes the corresponding density, the observed-data likelihood is
\begin{align*}
  L_n(\mu_i, \pi_i, \Psi)
  = \prod_{i = 1}^n
    f\left(Y_i; \mu_i, \Psi\right)^{A_i}
    \pi_i^{A_i}
    \left(1 - \pi_i\right)^{1 - A_i}.
\end{align*}
This factorizes as
\begin{align*}
  L_n(\mu_i, \pi_i, \Psi)
  = L_{Y,n}(\mu_i, \Psi)L_{A,n}(\pi_i),
\end{align*}
where
\begin{align*}
  L_{Y,n}(\mu_i, \Psi)
  &= \prod_{i = 1}^n f\left(Y_i; \mu_i, \Psi\right)^{A_i},\\
  L_{A,n}(\pi_i)
  &= \prod_{i = 1}^n \pi_i^{A_i}\left(1 - \pi_i\right)^{1 - A_i}.
\end{align*}
The factorization follows from the likelihood construction; it does not require independence between the value of $Y_i$ and the event $A_i = 1$.

We now impose finite-dimensional regression models on the two components. Let $s_\beta(x) = h_\beta(x)^\top\eta_\beta$ and $s_\mu(x) = h_\mu(x)^\top\eta_\mu$, where $h_\beta$ and $h_\mu$ are prespecified transformations of the baseline covariates and $\eta_\beta,\eta_\mu$ are nuisance parameters. The working models used for covariate adjustment are
\begin{align}
  \logit \Pr(A_i = 1 \mid R_i, X_i)
  &= \beta_0 + \beta_\delta R_i + s_\beta(X_i),\label{eq:glm1}\\
  \E[Y_i \mid A_i = 1, R_i, X_i]
  &= \mu_0 + \mu_\delta R_i + s_\mu(X_i),\label{eq:glm2}
\end{align}
so that treatment enters additively on the logit scale for the observation probability and additively on the mean scale for the continuous non-atom outcome. The parameter $\mu_\delta$ is the conditional mean difference among observed outcomes at fixed covariates, and $\beta_\delta$ is the conditional log odds-ratio of being observed. We write $\alpha_\delta = \exp(\beta_\delta)$ for the corresponding odds ratio.

On the original outcome scale, $\mu_\delta$ quantifies how treatment shifts the positive part of the outcome distribution among subjects with observed outcomes, whereas $\beta_\delta$ quantifies how treatment changes the odds that the outcome is observed at all. Reporting the pair therefore separates improvement in the continuous component from improvement in the probability of escaping the atom. This two-component contrast is the scientific target of the proposed likelihood-ratio test. It remains informative even when a single combined-outcome contrast such as $\Delta_{\widetilde{Y}}(X_i)$ is zero because the two components offset each other.

For fixed treatment contrasts $(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)$, the profile likelihood-ratio statistic is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:LRTtotal}
\begin{aligned}
  W_n^P(\mu_\delta, \beta_\delta)
  &= W_{Y,n}^P(\mu_\delta) + W_{A,n}^P(\beta_\delta),\\
  W_{Y,n}^P(\mu_\delta)
  &= -2\log \frac{\sup\limits_{\mu_0, s_\mu, \Psi} L_{Y,n}(\mu_0, \mu_\delta, s_\mu, \Psi)}
  {\sup\limits_{\mu_0, \mu_\delta, s_\mu, \Psi} L_{Y,n}(\mu_0, \mu_\delta, s_\mu, \Psi)},\\
  W_{A,n}^P(\beta_\delta)
  &= -2\log \frac{\sup\limits_{\beta_0, s_\beta} L_{A,n}(\beta_0, \beta_\delta, s_\beta)}
  {\sup\limits_{\beta_0, \beta_\delta, s_\beta} L_{A,n}(\beta_0, \beta_\delta, s_\beta)}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The null hypothesis of no treatment effect is tested with $W_n^P(0,0)$.

\subsection{Parametric likelihood-ratio test}

In the parametric version, the continuous non-atom outcome distribution is specified fully. In the Gaussian working model considered here,
\begin{align}
  A_i \mid R_i = r, X_i = x
  &\sim \Bernoulli\{\pi_r(x)\},\nonumber\\
  Y_i \mid A_i = 1, R_i = r, X_i = x
  &\sim N\{\mu_r(x),\sigma^2\},\label{eq:parametric-adjusted-model}
\end{align}
where
\[
  \pi_r(x)
  =
  \logit^{-1}\{\beta_0 + \beta_\delta r + s_\beta(x)\},
  \qquad
  \mu_r(x)
  =
  \mu_0 + \mu_\delta r + s_\mu(x),
  \qquad r \in \{0,1\}.
\]
The residual variance $\sigma^2$ is common to the two treatment arms, conditional on the adjustment covariates. Let $\theta_P = (\beta_0,\beta_\delta,\eta_\beta^\top,\mu_0,\mu_\delta,\eta_\mu^\top,\sigma^2)^\top$ collect all finite-dimensional parameters. The observed-data likelihood under the Gaussian model is
\begin{align}
  L_n^P(\theta_P)
  &=
  \prod_{i = 1}^n
  \left[
    \pi_{R_i}(X_i)^{A_i}
    \{1-\pi_{R_i}(X_i)\}^{1-A_i}
  \right]\nonumber\\
  &\quad \times
  \prod_{i = 1}^n
  \left[
    (2\pi\sigma^2)^{-1/2}
    \exp\left\{-\frac{\{Y_i-\mu_{R_i}(X_i)\}^2}{2\sigma^2}\right\}
  \right]^{A_i}.
  \label{eq:parametric-adjusted-likelihood}
\end{align}
Thus the likelihood is the product of a Bernoulli likelihood for whether the outcome escapes the atom and a Normal likelihood for the observed continuous values. The null model for the joint test imposes $\mu_\delta = 0$ and $\beta_\delta = 0$; the remaining regression parameters and $\sigma^2$ are nuisance parameters profiled out in Equation~(\ref{eq:LRTtotal}).

The regression formulation also induces marginal summaries standardized to a common covariate distribution. Let $\mathbb{P}_n$ denote the empirical distribution of the observed baseline covariates, so that $\mathbb{P}_n g(X) = n^{-1}\sum_{i=1}^n g(X_i)$. For $r \in \{0,1\}$ define
\begin{align}
  \bar{\pi}_{r,n}
  &=
  \mathbb{P}_n\{\pi_r(X)\},
  &
  \bar{\rho}_{r,n}
  &=
  1-\bar{\pi}_{r,n},
  &
  \bar{\mu}_{r,n}
  &=
  \mathbb{P}_n\{\mu_r(X)\}.
  \label{eq:standardized-components}
\end{align}
Here $\bar{\pi}_{r,n}$ is the observation probability that would be obtained if all subjects were assigned treatment arm $r$ while retaining the empirical covariate distribution, $\bar{\rho}_{r,n}$ is the corresponding atom probability, and $\bar{\mu}_{r,n}$ is the standardized mean of the continuous outcome among non-atom observations. The component contrasts are
\[
  \delta_{\pi,n} = \bar{\pi}_{1,n}-\bar{\pi}_{0,n},
  \qquad
  \delta_{\rho,n} = \bar{\rho}_{1,n}-\bar{\rho}_{0,n} = -\delta_{\pi,n},
  \qquad
  \delta_{\mu,n} = \bar{\mu}_{1,n}-\bar{\mu}_{0,n}.
\]
Under the additive mean model in Equation~(\ref{eq:glm2}), $\delta_{\mu,n} = \mu_\delta$ for every empirical covariate distribution. By contrast, the observation-probability contrast $\delta_{\pi,n}$ is a marginal standardization of the conditional log-odds model, whereas $\beta_\delta$ and $\alpha_\delta=\exp(\beta_\delta)$ remain the conditional log odds-ratio and odds ratio, respectively.

For a real-valued representation $c_{e_0}$ of the atom, the covariate-standardized combined-outcome contrast induced by the parametric model is
\begin{align}
  \Delta_n^{(e_0)}
  &=
  \mathbb{P}_n
  \Bigl[
    e_0\{1-\pi_1(X)\}
    + \pi_1(X)\mu_1(X)
    - e_0\{1-\pi_0(X)\}
    - \pi_0(X)\mu_0(X)
  \Bigr].
  \label{eq:standardized-delta}
\end{align}
For the atom-at-zero representation used in most of the manuscript, Equation~(\ref{eq:standardized-delta}) reduces to $\mathbb{P}_n\{\pi_1(X)\mu_1(X)-\pi_0(X)\mu_0(X)\}$. These standardized quantities compare the two treatment arms at the same empirical covariate distribution, rather than comparing arm-specific fitted means over potentially different covariate distributions.

\begin{proposition}
Assume that the parametric model for $Y_i \mid A_i = 1, R_i, X_i$ is correctly specified, that the regression models in Equations~(\ref{eq:glm1}) and~(\ref{eq:glm2}) are finite-dimensional and correctly specified, that the true parameter lies in the interior of the parameter space, that the model is identifiable with nonsingular Fisher information, and that $0 < \Pr(A_i = 1 \mid R_i = r, X_i) < 1$ almost surely for $r = 0,1$. Let $n_r = \sum_{i = 1}^n \mathbbm{1}(R_i = r)$ and assume $n_0, n_1 \to \infty$ with $n_1 / n_0 \to c \in (0,\infty)$. Then, under the null hypothesis $\mu_\delta = \beta_\delta = 0$, the profile likelihood-ratio statistic $W_n^P(0,0)$ in Equation~(\ref{eq:LRTtotal}) converges in distribution to $\chi^2_2$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Under the stated regularity conditions, the joint likelihood is a regular finite-dimensional likelihood with two scalar restrictions under the null: one in the continuous component and one in the Bernoulli component. Because the likelihood factorizes into $L_{Y,n}$ and $L_{A,n}$, the joint likelihood-ratio statistic is exactly the sum of the corresponding component likelihood-ratio statistics. Wilks' theorem \citep{wilks1938large} therefore yields the $\chi^2_2$ limit.
\end{proof}

The same fitted conditional laws determine both the component likelihood-ratio statistic and the standardized summaries in Equations~(\ref{eq:standardized-components})--(\ref{eq:standardized-delta}). Thus covariate adjustment has two distinct roles: it defines the conditional restrictions tested by the likelihood ratio, and it yields marginal summaries by averaging the fitted arm-specific conditional quantities over a common empirical distribution of $X_i$.

\subsection{Semi-parametric likelihood-ratio test}

The parametric test depends on the chosen distributional model for the observed outcomes. To relax that assumption, we replace the Gaussian likelihood for the continuous non-atom outcome by an empirical likelihood for the conditional mean restrictions, while retaining the parametric Bernoulli likelihood for $A_i$. The observation component is therefore still governed by Equation~(\ref{eq:glm1}), but no Normality, homoscedasticity, or parametric shape assumption is imposed on the distribution of $Y_i \mid A_i = 1, R_i, X_i$ beyond the finite-dimensional conditional mean model in Equation~(\ref{eq:glm2}).

Let $\mathcal{J} = \{i : A_i = 1\}$ and $M_n = |\mathcal{J}|$. For $i \in \mathcal{J}$ define the observed-outcome design vector
\[
  Z_i =
  \{1, R_i, h_\mu(X_i)^\top\}^\top,
  \qquad
  \theta =
  (\mu_0,\mu_\delta,\eta_\mu^\top)^\top,
\]
so that $\mu_{R_i}(X_i)=Z_i^\top\theta$. The semi-parametric continuous component is characterized by the moment restriction
\begin{equation}
  \E\!\left[
    Z_i\{Y_i-Z_i^\top\theta\}
    \mid A_i = 1
  \right] = 0.
  \label{eq:adjusted-el-moment}
\end{equation}
For a fixed candidate value $m_\delta$ of the treatment mean coefficient, write $\theta(m_\delta,\nu)$ for the vector obtained by setting the treatment component of $\theta$ equal to $m_\delta$ and collecting the remaining coefficients in the nuisance vector $\nu$. Define
\[
  g_i\{m_\delta,\nu\}
  =
  Z_i\left[Y_i-Z_i^\top\theta(m_\delta,\nu)\right],
  \qquad i \in \mathcal{J}.
\]
The profile empirical likelihood ratio for the continuous component is
\begin{align}
  R_{Y,n}^{\mathrm{E}}(m_\delta)
  =
  \sup_{\nu,\{p_i:i\in\mathcal{J}\}}
  \left\{
    \prod_{i \in \mathcal{J}} M_n p_i
  \right\},
  \label{eq:adjusted-el-primal}
\end{align}
where the supremum is over weights satisfying
\[
  p_i \geq 0,
  \qquad
  \sum_{i \in \mathcal{J}} p_i = 1,
  \qquad
  \sum_{i \in \mathcal{J}} p_i g_i\{m_\delta,\nu\}=0.
\]
The corresponding empirical-likelihood deviance is
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde{W}_{Y,n}^P(m_\delta)
  =
  -2\log R_{Y,n}^{\mathrm{E}}(m_\delta).
  \label{eq:adjusted-el-profile}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, for each feasible $(m_\delta,\nu)$ there is a Lagrange multiplier $\lambda(m_\delta,\nu)$ of the same dimension as $Z_i$ such that
\[
  \sum_{i \in \mathcal{J}}
  \frac{g_i\{m_\delta,\nu\}}
  {1+\lambda(m_\delta,\nu)^\top g_i\{m_\delta,\nu\}}
  =0,
  \qquad
  1+\lambda(m_\delta,\nu)^\top g_i\{m_\delta,\nu\} > 0
  \quad\text{for all }i\in\mathcal{J}.
\]
In dual form,
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde{W}_{Y,n}^P(m_\delta)
  =
  \inf_{\nu}
  2\sum_{i \in \mathcal{J}}
  \log\left[
    1+\lambda(m_\delta,\nu)^\top g_i\{m_\delta,\nu\}
  \right],
  \label{eq:adjusted-el-dual}
\end{equation}
with the value set to infinity when the empirical-likelihood constraints are infeasible. The fitted non-atom mean functions are standardized in the same way as in Equation~(\ref{eq:standardized-components}):
\[
  \bar{\mu}_{r,n}^{\mathrm{SP}}(\theta)
  =
  \mathbb{P}_n\{(1,r,h_\mu(X)^\top)\theta\},
  \qquad r\in\{0,1\}.
\]
Under the additive mean model, $\bar{\mu}_{1,n}^{\mathrm{SP}}(\theta)-\bar{\mu}_{0,n}^{\mathrm{SP}}(\theta)=\mu_\delta$. The semi-parametric standardized combined-outcome contrast is obtained by inserting this empirical-likelihood mean model and the fitted logistic observation probabilities into Equation~(\ref{eq:standardized-delta}). Thus the continuous distribution itself is left unspecified, but the mean component entering the standardized contrast is defined by the estimating equations in Equation~(\ref{eq:adjusted-el-moment}).

When $X_i$ is absent, the empirical-likelihood regression construction reduces to the familiar two-sample empirical likelihood for the observed outcome means. Let
\[
\mathcal{I}_0 = \{i = 1,\ldots,n : R_i = 0, A_i = 1\},
\qquad
\mathcal{I}_1 = \{i = 1,\ldots,n : R_i = 1, A_i = 1\}.
\]
Lemma~\ref{lem:empirical-likelihood-derivation} in Appendix~\ref{app:supplementary-derivation} shows that, for fixed $(\mu_0,\mu_\delta)$, the empirical-likelihood deviance for the observed outcomes is
\begin{align}
  \widetilde{W}_{Y,n}(\mu_0,\mu_\delta)
  &= 2\sum_{i = 1}^n \Biggl[
    (1-R_i)\log\left(1 + \lambda_0(\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0} - A_i\mu_0)\right)
    + R_i\log\left(1 + \lambda_1(\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0} - A_i(\mu_0 + \mu_\delta))\right)
  \Biggr],\label{eq:nonparametric}
\end{align}
where terms with $A_i = 0$ contribute zero because $\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0} = 0$ in that case. Writing
\[
  n_0 = \sum_{i = 1}^n (1-R_i)A_i = |\mathcal{I}_0|,
  \qquad
  n_1 = \sum_{i = 1}^n R_iA_i = |\mathcal{I}_1|,
\]
the dual parameters $\lambda_0$ and $\lambda_1$ solve
\begin{align*}
  \frac{1}{n_0}\sum_{i = 1}^n
  (1-R_i)\frac{\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0} - A_i\mu_0}
  {1 + \lambda_0(\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0} - A_i\mu_0)} &= 0,\\
  \frac{1}{n_1}\sum_{i = 1}^n
  R_i\frac{\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0} - A_i(\mu_0 + \mu_\delta)}
  {1 + \lambda_1(\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0} - A_i(\mu_0 + \mu_\delta))} &= 0.
\end{align*}
The profile version is obtained by minimizing over the nuisance mean:
\begin{align*}
  \widetilde{W}_{Y,n}^P(\mu_\delta) = \inf_{\mu_0 \in \mathbb{R}} \widetilde{W}_{Y,n}(\mu_0,\mu_\delta).
\end{align*}
The admissible values of $\mu_0$ are those for which the corresponding constrained means lie in the convex hull of the observed outcomes in each group. For interior feasible means, the dual parameters $\lambda_0$ and $\lambda_1$ are determined by one-dimensional root equations with unique roots on their admissible intervals, and the profiled nuisance mean is then minimized over its feasible set. If a candidate constraint falls outside the convex-hull region, the corresponding empirical-likelihood statistic is treated as infinite.

The semi-parametric test statistic is then
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde{W}_n^P(\mu_\delta, \beta_\delta)
  = \widetilde{W}_{Y,n}^P(\mu_\delta) + W_{A,n}^P(\beta_\delta).\label{eq:LRT_SP_total}
\end{equation}
The first term is the profile empirical-likelihood deviance in Equation~(\ref{eq:adjusted-el-profile}), or equivalently Equation~(\ref{eq:nonparametric}) in the no-covariate special case; the second term is the Bernoulli profile likelihood-ratio statistic from Equation~(\ref{eq:LRTtotal}).

\begin{proposition}[Covariate-adjusted semi-parametric likelihood-ratio test]\label{prop:covariate-adjusted-splrt}
Consider the null hypothesis
\[
  H_0:\ \mu_\delta = 0,\quad \beta_\delta = 0,
\]
equivalently, under the additive adjustment models, no covariate-standardized difference in the non-atom mean and no covariate-specific log odds-ratio for observation. Assume that $(Y_i,A_i,R_i,X_i)$ are independent observations from a common law; that for some $\varepsilon>0$, $\varepsilon \leq \Pr(R_i=1\mid X_i) \leq 1-\varepsilon$ and $\varepsilon \leq \Pr(A_i=1\mid R_i=r,X_i) \leq 1-\varepsilon$ almost surely for $r=0,1$; that the logistic model in Equation~(\ref{eq:glm1}) and the conditional mean model in Equation~(\ref{eq:glm2}) are correctly specified; that the true finite-dimensional parameters lie in the interior of their parameter spaces; that the Fisher information for the Bernoulli component is nonsingular; and that, for the empirical-likelihood component, $\E\|Z_i\{Y_i-Z_i^\top\theta_0\}\|^{2+\eta}<\infty$ for some $\eta>0$, the covariance matrix of $Z_i\{Y_i-Z_i^\top\theta_0\}$ conditional on $A_i=1$ is nonsingular, and the derivative matrix of the moment condition in Equation~(\ref{eq:adjusted-el-moment}) has full column rank. Then, under $H_0$,
\[
  \widetilde{W}_n^P(0,0)
  =
  \widetilde{W}_{Y,n}^P(0)+W_{A,n}^P(0)
  \ \overset{d}{\longrightarrow}\ \chi^2_2.
\]
The two degrees of freedom correspond to the two scalar restrictions tested jointly: $\mu_\delta=0$ in the continuous mean component and $\beta_\delta=0$ in the observation component.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The Bernoulli component is a regular finite-dimensional likelihood with one scalar restriction, so Wilks' theorem gives $W_{A,n}^P(0)\overset{d}{\to}\chi^2_1$. Conditional on the non-atom sample, the continuous component is an empirical likelihood for estimating equations with a single profiled scalar restriction. The empirical-likelihood Wilks theorem for estimating equations therefore gives $\widetilde{W}_{Y,n}^P(0)\overset{d}{\to}\chi^2_1$ under the stated moment, rank, and interiority conditions \citep{owen1991empirical,qin1994empirical}. The Bernoulli score is a function of $(A_i,R_i,X_i)$, whereas the empirical-likelihood estimating function has conditional mean zero given $(A_i=1,R_i,X_i)$; hence the two components are asymptotically block-orthogonal. The sum in Equation~(\ref{eq:LRT_SP_total}) therefore has the $\chi^2_2$ null limit.
\end{proof}

The adjusted parametric and semi-parametric analyses consequently target the same two-component treatment structure. Both use the logistic model for the probability of escaping the atom and both summarize treatment effects at the empirical covariate distribution through Equations~(\ref{eq:standardized-components})--(\ref{eq:standardized-delta}). They differ in the continuous non-atom component: the parametric analysis uses a fully specified Gaussian conditional distribution with common residual variance, whereas the semi-parametric analysis uses only the regression moment restrictions in Equation~(\ref{eq:adjusted-el-moment}) and leaves the remaining shape of the continuous distribution unspecified. The semi-parametric likelihood-ratio test is therefore less sensitive to non-Normality of the continuous outcomes, at the cost of relying more directly on large-sample empirical-likelihood approximations and on feasibility of the moment constraints.

\subsection{Inference and interpretation}

Confidence regions follow by inverting the corresponding likelihood-ratio statistic. For the parametric procedure, an asymptotic $(1-\alpha)100\%$ simultaneous confidence region for $(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)$ is
\begin{align*}
  \mathrm{CI}^{(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)}_{1-\alpha}
  = \left\{(m,b) \in \mathbb{R}^2 : W_n^P(m,b) \leq \chi^2_{2,1-\alpha}\right\}.
\end{align*}
For the semi-parametric procedure, one replaces $W_n^P$ by $\widetilde{W}_n^P$. Correspondingly, the marginal intervals are
\begin{align*}
  \mathrm{CI}^{\mu_\delta}_{1-\alpha}
  &= \left\{m \in \mathbb{R} : W_{Y,n}^P(m) \leq \chi^2_{1,1-\alpha}\right\}
  \quad \text{or} \quad
  \left\{m \in \mathbb{R} : \widetilde{W}_{Y,n}^P(m) \leq \chi^2_{1,1-\alpha}\right\},\\
  \mathrm{CI}^{\beta_\delta}_{1-\alpha}
  &= \left\{b \in \mathbb{R} : W_{A,n}^P(b) \leq \chi^2_{1,1-\alpha}\right\}.
\end{align*}
Here $\chi^2_{q,1-\alpha}$ denotes the $(1-\alpha)$ quantile of a chi-square distribution with $q$ degrees of freedom. We recommend reporting the simultaneous confidence region because it displays the joint uncertainty about the effect on the continuous and binary components.

Many applied reports also require an interval on the numerical combined-outcome scale. Such an interval concerns a derived scalar functional, denoted here by $\Delta$ or $\Delta^{(e_0)}$, rather than the two-component parameter itself. It therefore answers a different inferential question from the region for $(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)$: it summarizes the consequence of the atom and continuous components after the atom has been assigned the numerical value $e_0$ through the representation map $c_{e_0}$. This scalar summary is useful for reporting effects on the original numerical scale, but it is secondary to the two-component analysis because it necessarily collapses changes in the observation probability and changes in the non-atom mean into a single number.

To define the likelihood-based intervals uniformly, let
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{W}_n(m,b)
  =
  \begin{cases}
    W_n^P(m,b), & \text{for the parametric likelihood},\\[0.4em]
    \widetilde{W}_n^P(m,b), & \text{for the semi-parametric likelihood}.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:generic-lr-surface}
\end{equation}
For fixed component contrasts $(m,b)$, let $\widehat{\pi}_r(x;b)$ denote the fitted observation probability in arm $r$ obtained after profiling the Bernoulli nuisance parameters under the restriction $\beta_\delta=b$. Similarly, let $\widehat{\mu}_r(x;m)$ denote the fitted conditional non-atom mean in arm $r$ obtained after profiling the continuous-component nuisance parameters under the restriction $\mu_\delta=m$. In the parametric analysis this profiling is with respect to the Gaussian likelihood; in the semi-parametric analysis it is with respect to the empirical-likelihood estimating-equation constraints. The profiled combined-outcome contrast induced by $(m,b)$ and the atom representation $c_{e_0}$ is
\begin{align}
  g_n^{(e_0)}(m,b)
  &=
  \mathbb{P}_n
  \Bigl[
    e_0\{1-\widehat{\pi}_1(X;b)\}
    + \widehat{\pi}_1(X;b)\widehat{\mu}_1(X;m)
    \nonumber\\
  &\qquad\qquad
    - e_0\{1-\widehat{\pi}_0(X;b)\}
    - \widehat{\pi}_0(X;b)\widehat{\mu}_0(X;m)
  \Bigr].
  \label{eq:profiled-delta-map}
\end{align}
When there are no adjustment covariates and $e_0=0$, this reduces to
\[
  g_n^{(0)}(m,b)
  =
  \widehat{\pi}_1(b)\widehat{\mu}_1(m)
  -
  \widehat{\pi}_0(b)\widehat{\mu}_0(m),
\]
where $\widehat{\pi}_r(b)$ and $\widehat{\mu}_r(m)$ are the corresponding arm-specific profiled probabilities and means. The fitted likelihood-based estimate of the numerical combined-outcome contrast is $g_n^{(e_0)}(\widehat{\mu}_\delta,\widehat{\beta}_\delta)$, where $(\widehat{\mu}_\delta,\widehat{\beta}_\delta)$ denotes the unrestricted component estimate.

The first interval is the direct Welch interval for the atom-at-zero numerical outcome. Let $n_r=\sum_{i=1}^n\mathbbm{1}(R_i=r)$ and define
\[
  \widehat{\Delta}_{\mathrm{W}}
  =
  \bar{\widetilde{Y}}^{\,0}_1-\bar{\widetilde{Y}}^{\,0}_0,
  \qquad
  \bar{\widetilde{Y}}^{\,0}_r
  =
  n_r^{-1}\sum_{i:R_i=r}\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0},
\]
with arm-specific sample variances
\[
  S_r^2
  =
  (n_r-1)^{-1}\sum_{i:R_i=r}
  \left(\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0}-\bar{\widetilde{Y}}^{\,0}_r\right)^2,
  \qquad r\in\{0,1\}.
\]
The Welch standard error and Satterthwaite degrees of freedom are
\[
  \widehat{\mathrm{se}}_{\mathrm{W}}^2
  =
  \frac{S_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{S_0^2}{n_0},
  \qquad
  \widehat{\nu}
  =
  \frac{
    \left(S_1^2/n_1+S_0^2/n_0\right)^2
  }{
    \frac{(S_1^2/n_1)^2}{n_1-1}
    +
    \frac{(S_0^2/n_0)^2}{n_0-1}
  },
\]
with the usual limiting interpretation if one of the variance terms is zero. The resulting interval is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CI}^{\Delta,\mathrm{W}}_{1-\alpha}
  =
  \left[
    \widehat{\Delta}_{\mathrm{W}}
    -
    t_{\widehat{\nu},1-\alpha/2}\widehat{\mathrm{se}}_{\mathrm{W}},
    \widehat{\Delta}_{\mathrm{W}}
    +
    t_{\widehat{\nu},1-\alpha/2}\widehat{\mathrm{se}}_{\mathrm{W}}
  \right],
  \label{eq:welch-delta-ci}
\end{equation}
where $t_{\widehat{\nu},1-\alpha/2}$ is the corresponding Student quantile. This interval treats $\widetilde{Y}^{\,0}$ as an ordinary real-valued outcome and therefore does not use the two-component likelihood structure.

The second interval is obtained by projecting the simultaneous likelihood-ratio confidence region for the component parameter onto the $\Delta^{(e_0)}$ scale. Define
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{C}_{2,n}(1-\alpha)
  =
  \left\{
    (m,b)\in\mathbb{R}^2:
    \mathcal{W}_n(m,b)\leq\chi^2_{2,1-\alpha}
  \right\}.
  \label{eq:component-simultaneous-region}
\end{equation}
The projected likelihood-ratio interval is the image of this region under the profiled map in Equation~(\ref{eq:profiled-delta-map}):
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CI}^{\Delta,\mathrm{proj}}_{1-\alpha}
  =
  \left[
    \inf_{(m,b)\in\mathcal{C}_{2,n}(1-\alpha)}
    g_n^{(e_0)}(m,b),
    \sup_{(m,b)\in\mathcal{C}_{2,n}(1-\alpha)}
    g_n^{(e_0)}(m,b)
  \right].
  \label{eq:projected-delta-ci}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, its endpoints are the constrained optimization problems
\begin{align}
  L_{\mathrm{proj}}
  &=
  \inf_{m,b}
  g_n^{(e_0)}(m,b)
  \quad
  \text{subject to}
  \quad
  \mathcal{W}_n(m,b)\leq\chi^2_{2,1-\alpha},
  \label{eq:projected-delta-lower}\\
  U_{\mathrm{proj}}
  &=
  \sup_{m,b}
  g_n^{(e_0)}(m,b)
  \quad
  \text{subject to}
  \quad
  \mathcal{W}_n(m,b)\leq\chi^2_{2,1-\alpha}.
  \label{eq:projected-delta-upper}
\end{align}
Because this construction preserves the entire two-dimensional confidence set before mapping it to a scalar, it inherits simultaneous coverage for $(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)$ and is typically conservative for $\Delta^{(e_0)}$ alone.

The third interval profiles the likelihood ratio directly over the scalar constraint $\Delta^{(e_0)}=d$. For each $d\in\mathbb{R}$, define
\begin{equation}
  T_n(d)
  =
  \inf_{m,b}
  \left\{
    \mathcal{W}_n(m,b):
    g_n^{(e_0)}(m,b)=d
  \right\}.
  \label{eq:delta-profile-stat-section}
\end{equation}
Thus evaluating $T_n(d)$ is itself a constrained optimization problem: minimize the joint likelihood-ratio surface over component contrasts whose profiled numerical combined-outcome contrast is exactly $d$. The profile likelihood-ratio interval is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CI}^{\Delta,\mathrm{prof}}_{1-\alpha}
  =
  \left\{
    d\in\mathbb{R}: T_n(d)\leq\chi^2_{1,1-\alpha}
  \right\},
  \label{eq:profile-delta-ci}
\end{equation}
with endpoints
\[
  L_{\mathrm{prof}}
  =
  \inf\{d:T_n(d)\leq\chi^2_{1,1-\alpha}\},
  \qquad
  U_{\mathrm{prof}}
  =
  \sup\{d:T_n(d)\leq\chi^2_{1,1-\alpha}\}.
\]
The one-degree-of-freedom calibration reflects that the null constraint is on the single scalar functional $g_n^{(e_0)}(m,b)$, while the remaining component direction is profiled out.

\begin{proposition}[Likelihood-ratio intervals for the combined-outcome contrast]\label{prop:delta-lr-intervals}
Let $\theta_\delta=(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)^\top$. Suppose that $\mathcal{W}_n$ satisfies the regularity conditions for the relevant parametric or semi-parametric likelihood-ratio theory in a neighborhood of the true value $\theta_{\delta0}$. Assume further that $g_n^{(e_0)}$ converges uniformly in probability on this neighborhood to a continuously differentiable population map $g^{(e_0)}$, that $\nabla g^{(e_0)}(\theta_{\delta0})\neq 0$, and that $\Delta_0=g^{(e_0)}(\theta_{\delta0})$. Then
\[
  T_n(\Delta_0)\ \overset{d}{\longrightarrow}\ \chi^2_1,
\]
and the profile interval in Equation~(\ref{eq:profile-delta-ci}) has asymptotic coverage $1-\alpha$ for $\Delta_0$. Moreover, if $\mathcal{C}_{2,n}(1-\alpha)$ is an asymptotic $(1-\alpha)100\%$ confidence region for $\theta_{\delta0}$, then the projected interval in Equation~(\ref{eq:projected-delta-ci}) has at least asymptotic coverage $1-\alpha$ for $\Delta_0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The likelihood-ratio surface is locally quadratic under the regularity conditions for the corresponding parametric likelihood or empirical-likelihood estimating-equation model. Since $g^{(e_0)}$ is a smooth scalar map with nonzero gradient at $\theta_{\delta0}$, the null hypothesis $g^{(e_0)}(\theta_\delta)=\Delta_0$ imposes one regular scalar restriction on the two-component parameter. Wilks' theorem and the empirical-likelihood analogue therefore give the $\chi^2_1$ limit for the profiled statistic. For the projected interval, the event $\theta_{\delta0}\in\mathcal{C}_{2,n}(1-\alpha)$ implies, by construction and continuity of $g_n^{(e_0)}$, that $g_n^{(e_0)}(\theta_{\delta0})$ lies in the image interval up to an asymptotically negligible error. The stated coverage follows from the coverage of the two-dimensional confidence region.
\end{proof}

The three intervals therefore describe related but distinct targets. The Welch interval in Equation~(\ref{eq:welch-delta-ci}) is a direct one-dimensional interval for the mean difference of the coded numerical outcome $\widetilde{Y}^{\,0}$; it is simple and relatively model-light, but it does not distinguish whether the contrast arises from the atom probability, the continuous non-atom component, or both. The projected interval in Equation~(\ref{eq:projected-delta-ci}) is a likelihood-based summary derived from the simultaneous two-component confidence region; it is appropriate when $\Delta^{(e_0)}$ is reported as a secondary scalar summary while maintaining coherence with the joint analysis, and it is usually wider because it retains simultaneous uncertainty in both $\mu_\delta$ and $\beta_\delta$. The profile interval in Equation~(\ref{eq:profile-delta-ci}) is instead a direct likelihood-ratio interval for the scalar functional $\Delta^{(e_0)}$; it profiles over nuisance component directions and is therefore more focused on the numerical combined-outcome contrast. Thus the profile and projected intervals remain tied to the two-part likelihood decomposition, whereas the Welch interval is an analysis of the numerical representation alone.

\subsection{Bayesian two-group comparison}\label{sec:bayesian-two-group}

The preceding likelihood-based development admits a natural Bayesian counterpart. We again suppress $X_i$ and work in the no-covariate setting, because this is the clearest setting in which to formulate the two-part observed-data law itself. Let $\widetilde{Y}_i^{(e_0)} = c_{e_0}(\widetilde{Y}_i)$ denote the numerical representation of the combined outcome for a fixed atom value $e_0 \in \mathbb{R}$. In the two-group comparison without covariates, the primary objects are the arm-specific observed-data laws. It is therefore more natural to fit treatment-stratified models than to impose a common mixture across treatment arms. A common mixture with only arm-specific shifts or scales would encode a priori similarity in distributional shape across arms, whereas in the present setting treatment may affect both the atom probability and the shape of the continuous non-atom distribution. Separate arm-specific mixtures align directly with the scientific question of whether the full observed-data law differs between treatment groups.

Let $\mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ denote the support of the continuous non-atom outcomes on the chosen numerical scale. For continuous-support models, the examples below use $\mathcal{C} = \mathbb{R}$ or $\mathcal{C} = (0,\infty)$; bounded reported-score models replace the continuous conditional density by a probability mass function on a finite score set. Let $\lambda_{\mathcal{C}}$ denote Lebesgue measure on $\mathcal{C}$, and define the dominating measure
\[
  \nu_{e_0} = \delta_{e_0} + \lambda_{\mathcal{C}}.
\]
For each treatment arm $r \in \{0,1\}$, we write the law of the numerical combined outcome as
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}(\widetilde{Y}_i^{(e_0)} \mid R_i = r)
  = \rho_r \delta_{e_0} + \pi_r F_r^c,
  \qquad
  \pi_r = 1 - \rho_r,
  \qquad
  F_r^c \ll \lambda_{\mathcal{C}},
  \label{eq:bayes-two-part}
\end{equation}
where $\rho_r = \Pr(\widetilde{Y}_i^{(e_0)} = e_0 \mid R_i = r)$ is the arm-specific atom probability and $F_r^c$ is an absolutely continuous probability measure on $\mathcal{C}$ describing the non-atom outcomes. Equivalently, with $f_r^c$ denoting the density of $F_r^c$ with respect to $\lambda_{\mathcal{C}}$, the density of $\widetilde{Y}_i^{(e_0)} \mid R_i = r$ with respect to $\nu_{e_0}$ is
\[
  q_r(y)
  = \rho_r \mathbbm{1}\{y = e_0\}
    + \pi_r f_r^c(y)\mathbbm{1}\{y \in \mathcal{C}\}.
\]
One could instead start directly from a single density with respect to the extended measure $\nu_{e_0}$ on $\mathcal{C} \cup \{e_0\}$. For the present problem, however, the explicit singular-plus-absolutely-continuous decomposition in Equation~(\ref{eq:bayes-two-part}) is clearer, because it preserves the atom probability and the non-atom distribution as separate scientific objects and mirrors the observed-data factorization used in the likelihood-based analysis.

The main modeling task is therefore to specify a flexible prior for $F_r^c$. This is precisely the setting in which a Dirichlet process mixture model is attractive. The conditional non-atom distribution may be skewed, heavy-tailed, or multimodal, and a single parametric family can be too restrictive even when the scientific summaries of interest remain simple. By modeling $F_r^c$ through a nonparametric mixture, one can accommodate complex distributional shape while retaining interpretable treatment-arm summaries such as the atom probability, the mean among non-atom outcomes, and contrasts derived from them.

Let $(\Theta,\mathcal{T})$ denote the parameter space for a support-matched kernel $K(\cdot \mid \theta)$ on $\mathcal{C}$. An arm-specific mixing distribution $G_r$ is said to follow a Dirichlet process with concentration parameter $\alpha_r > 0$ and base measure $G_0$, written $G_r \sim \mathrm{DP}(\alpha_r,G_0)$, if for every measurable partition $(B_1,\ldots,B_m)$ of $\Theta$,
\[
  \bigl(G_r(B_1),\ldots,G_r(B_m)\bigr)
  \sim
  \mathrm{Dirichlet}\bigl(\alpha_r G_0(B_1),\ldots,\alpha_r G_0(B_m)\bigr)
\]
\citep{ferguson1973bayesian}. When $G_r$ is used as a mixing distribution,
\[
  F_r^c(B) = \int_{\Theta} K(B \mid \theta)\,G_r(d\theta),
  \qquad B \subseteq \mathcal{C},
\]
which yields the familiar Dirichlet process mixture model \citep{antoniak1974mixtures}. By Sethuraman's stick-breaking representation \citep{sethuraman1994constructive},
\[
  G_r = \sum_{h=1}^{\infty} w_{rh}\delta_{\theta_{rh}},
  \qquad
  w_{r1} = v_{r1},
  \qquad
  w_{rh} = v_{rh}\prod_{\ell=1}^{h-1}(1-v_{r\ell}),
  \quad h \geq 2,
\]
with
\[
  v_{rh} \mid \alpha_r \sim \Beta(1,\alpha_r),
  \qquad
  \theta_{rh} \stackrel{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim} G_0.
\]
For computation we use the standard finite truncated approximation \citep{ishwaran2001gibbs}, retaining $H$ mixture components and assigning the remaining stick mass to the last retained component. This approximation gives a finite-dimensional posterior computation while retaining the stick-breaking interpretation.

The same two-part construction accommodates several choices of non-atom support by changing only the kernel $K(\cdot\mid\theta)$ and its base distribution. On the full real line one may use Normal kernels; on the positive real line, Gamma kernels parameterized by their component means; and on a bounded interval $[a,b]$, kernels defined after the affine transformation $x=(y-a)/(b-a)\in(0,1)$. For a continuously observed bounded outcome this gives a density on the original scale through the usual Jacobian. For a reported score outcome, let $\mathcal{S}=\{s_1,\ldots,s_J\}\subset[a,b]$ be the possible reported scores and let $q_\ell$, $\ell=1,\ldots,L$, denote candidate reporting-grid widths, such as one-, five-, or ten-point grids. If score $s_j$ is admissible on grid $\ell$, define the unit-scale interval
\[
  I_{\ell j}
  =
  [l_{\ell j},u_{\ell j}]
  =
  \left[
    \frac{\max(a,s_j-q_\ell/2)-a}{b-a},
    \frac{\min(b,s_j+q_\ell/2)-a}{b-a}
  \right],
\]
and set $c_{\ell j}=1$ for admissible score-grid pairs and $c_{\ell j}=0$ otherwise. Let $G_\theta$ be the CDF of a unit-interval kernel, for example a Beta CDF $G_\theta(x)=I_x\{m\phi,(1-m)\phi\}$ or a logit-Normal CDF $G_\theta(x)=\Phi\{(\operatorname{logit}x-\mu)/\sigma\}$. The component probability of observing score $s_j$ under reporting grid $\ell$ is then
\[
  q_{rh\ell j}(\theta_{rh})
  =
  c_{\ell j}
  \left\{
    G_{\theta_{rh}}(u_{\ell j}) - G_{\theta_{rh}}(l_{\ell j})
  \right\},
\]
with endpoint intervals truncated to $[0,1]$ and CDF values at 0 and 1 interpreted by continuity. If $\eta_{r\ell}$ are the reporting-grid weights in arm $r$, the arm-specific score probability is
\[
  p_{rj}(\vartheta)
  =
  \sum_{h=1}^{H} w_{rh}
  \sum_{\ell=1}^{L}\eta_{r\ell} q_{rh\ell j}(\theta_{rh}),
  \qquad
  \sum_{j=1}^{J}p_{rj}(\vartheta)=1.
\]
Thus, writing $d_r$ for the number of atom observations in arm $r$, $m_r$ for the number of non-atom observations, and $n_{rj}$ for the number of reported non-atom scores equal to $s_j$, the concrete likelihood for the heaped reported-score model is, up to multinomial constants,
\[
  L_{\mathrm{score}}(\vartheta)
  \propto
  \prod_{r=0}^{1}
  \rho_r^{d_r}\pi_r^{m_r}
  \prod_{j=1}^{J}
  p_{rj}(\vartheta)^{n_{rj}},
  \qquad
  \pi_r=1-\rho_r.
\]
The reporting-grid weights may be constrained to be common across arms, $\eta_{0\ell}=\eta_{1\ell}$, or allowed to vary by arm; either way the atom probability, mixture weights, and unit-interval kernel parameters retain the same interpretation.

Writing $\widetilde{Y}_i^{(e_0)} = e_0$ when $A_i = 0$ and $\widetilde{Y}_i^{(e_0)} = Y_i$ when $A_i = 1$, the no-covariate model may be expressed hierarchically as
\begin{align}
  A_i \mid R_i = r
  &\sim \Bernoulli(\pi_r),
  \qquad
  r \in \{0,1\},
  \nonumber\\
  \pi_r
  &= 1 - \rho_r,
  \qquad
  \rho_r \sim \Beta(a_{\rho}, b_{\rho}),
  \nonumber\\
  Y_i \mid (A_i = 1, R_i = r)
  &\sim F_r^c,
  \nonumber\\
  F_r^c(dy)
  &= \sum_{h=1}^{H} w_{rh}K(dy \mid \theta_{rh}),
  \nonumber\\
  v_{rh} \mid \alpha_r
  &\sim \Beta(1,\alpha_r),
  \qquad
  h = 1,\ldots,H-1,
  \nonumber\\
  \alpha_r
  &\sim \mathrm{Gamma}(a_{\alpha}, b_{\alpha}),
  \nonumber\\
  w_{r1}
  &= v_{r1},
  \nonumber\\
  w_{rh}
  &= v_{rh}\prod_{\ell=1}^{h-1}(1-v_{r\ell}),
  \qquad
  h = 2,\ldots,H-1,
  \nonumber\\
  w_{rH}
  &= \prod_{\ell=1}^{H-1}(1-v_{r\ell}),
  \label{eq:bayes-hierarchy}\\
  K(dy \mid \theta_{rh})
  &= \phi(y; m_{rh}, s_{rh}^2)\,dy,
  \qquad
  \mathcal{C} = \mathbb{R},
  \nonumber\\
  m_{rh}
  &\sim N(m_0,s_0^2),
  \qquad
  s_{rh} \sim \mathrm{Lognormal}(m_s,s_s^2),
  \nonumber\\
  K(dy \mid \theta_{rh})
  &= g\!\left(y; a_{rh}, a_{rh}/m_{rh}\right)\,dy,
  \qquad
  \mathcal{C} = (0,\infty),
  \nonumber\\
  m_{rh}
  &\sim \mathrm{Lognormal}(m_m,s_m^2),
  \qquad
  a_{rh} \sim \mathrm{Lognormal}(m_a,s_a^2),
  \nonumber\\
  K(\{s_j\}\mid \theta_{rh},\eta_r)
  &=
  \sum_{\ell=1}^{L}\eta_{r\ell}
  c_{\ell j}\{G_{\theta_{rh}}(u_{\ell j})-G_{\theta_{rh}}(l_{\ell j})\},
  \qquad
  \mathcal{C}=\{s_1,\ldots,s_J\},
  \nonumber\\
  G_{\theta_{rh}}(x)
  &=
  I_x\{m_{rh}\phi_{rh},(1-m_{rh})\phi_{rh}\},
  \nonumber\\
  m_{rh}
  &\sim \Beta(a_m,b_m),
  \qquad
  \phi_{rh}\sim \mathrm{Lognormal}(m_\phi,s_\phi^2),
  \nonumber\\
  G_{\theta_{rh}}(x)
  &=
  \Phi\{(\operatorname{logit}x-\mu_{rh})/\sigma_{rh}\},
  \qquad
  \mu_{r1}<\cdots<\mu_{rH},
  \qquad
  \sigma_{rh}>0,
  \nonumber\\
  \log\sigma_{rh}
  &\sim N(m_\sigma,s_\sigma^2),
  \qquad
  \eta_r \sim \mathrm{Dirichlet}(\zeta).
  \nonumber
\end{align}
where $\phi(\cdot; m,s^2)$ denotes the Normal density with mean $m$ and variance $s^2$, and $g(\cdot; a,b)$ denotes the Gamma density with shape $a$ and rate $b$. Thus the full-real-line member uses Normal kernels, whereas the positive-support member uses Gamma kernels parameterized so that $m_{rh}$ is the component mean. The latter formulation requires all non-atom outcomes to be strictly positive. For bounded outcomes, the unit-interval kernel may be either Beta or logit-Normal; for reported scores, the observed score probabilities are obtained by integrating the unit-interval kernel over the reporting intervals and averaging over the reporting grids. In all versions, the mixture is arm-specific, the atom probability is explicit, and the scientific summaries remain directly interpretable despite the flexibility of the non-atom component.

\paragraph{Posterior inference.}
Let $\mathcal{D}_n = \{(\widetilde{Y}_i^{(e_0)},A_i,R_i):i=1,\ldots,n\}$ denote the observed data, and let $\vartheta$ collect all unknown quantities in Equation~(\ref{eq:bayes-hierarchy}). For arm $r$, the posterior predictive distribution of a future numerical combined outcome $\widetilde{Y}_{r,\mathrm{new}}^{(e_0)}$ is
\begin{align*}
  \Pr\!\left(\widetilde{Y}_{r,\mathrm{new}}^{(e_0)} \in B \mid \mathcal{D}_n\right)
  &=
  \int
  \Biggl\{
    \rho_r \delta_{e_0}(B) \\
  &\qquad\qquad\qquad
    + \pi_r \int_{B \cap \mathcal{C}} f_r^c(y \mid \vartheta)\,\lambda_{\mathcal{C}}(dy)
  \Biggr\}
  p(\vartheta \mid \mathcal{D}_n)\,d\vartheta.
\end{align*}
where
\[
  f_r^c(y \mid \vartheta)
  = \sum_{h=1}^{H} w_{rh} k(y \mid \theta_{rh})
\]
is the arm-specific non-atom mixture density for continuous-support fits, with the analogous score probability mass function used for bounded-score fits. For posterior draw $m$, the corresponding mean among non-atom outcomes is
\[
  \mu_r^{c,(m)}
  = \int_{\mathcal{C}} y f_r^c(y \mid \vartheta^{(m)})\,\lambda_{\mathcal{C}}(dy),
\]
with the integral replaced by $\sum_j s_j p_{rj}^{(m)}$ for bounded-score fits. Under the Normal and Gamma kernels this reduces to the weighted component-mean average $\mu_r^{c,(m)} = \sum_{h=1}^{H} w_{rh}^{(m)} m_{rh}^{(m)}$. The analysis reports the arm-specific summaries $\rho_r$, $\pi_r$, and $\mu_r^c$ together with the treatment contrasts
\begin{align}
  \Delta_{\mathrm{atom}}
  &= \rho_1 - \rho_0,
  \qquad
  \mu_{\delta}
  = \mu_1^c - \mu_0^c,
  \qquad
  \alpha_{\delta}
  = \frac{\pi_1/(1-\pi_1)}{\pi_0/(1-\pi_0)},
  \nonumber\\
  \mu_r^{(e_0)}
  &= e_0\rho_r + \pi_r\mu_r^c,
  \qquad
  \Delta^{(e_0)}
  = \mu_1^{(e_0)} - \mu_0^{(e_0)}.
  \label{eq:bayes-estimands}
\end{align}
For the atom-at-zero representation used elsewhere in the manuscript, $\Delta = \Delta^{(0)} = \pi_1\mu_1^c - \pi_0\mu_0^c$. Thus the Bayesian analysis reports, on a common posterior scale, the atom probability in each arm, the mean among non-atom outcomes in each arm, the atom-probability contrast, the contrast in non-atom means, the odds-ratio summary $\alpha_{\delta}$ for escaping the atom, and the induced contrast on the numerical combined-outcome scale.

All of these functionals are computed draw-by-draw from the posterior sample and are then summarized by posterior medians or means, equal-tail credible intervals, and directional posterior probabilities. In the analyses below the default point summaries are posterior medians, with equal-tail credible intervals. Because several scientifically relevant quantities are nonlinear functions of $(\rho_r,\pi_r,\mu_r^c)$, it is important to compute them at each posterior draw rather than by plugging posterior means into the formulas in Equation~(\ref{eq:bayes-estimands}). Directional summaries such as $\Pr(\Delta_{\mathrm{atom}} < 0 \mid \mathcal{D}_n)$, $\Pr(\mu_{\delta} > 0 \mid \mathcal{D}_n)$, $\Pr(\alpha_{\delta} > 1 \mid \mathcal{D}_n)$, or $\Pr(\Delta^{(e_0)} > 0 \mid \mathcal{D}_n)$ are then immediate from the posterior draws, with the relevant direction chosen according to whether larger or smaller outcomes are clinically preferred.

\paragraph{Posterior predictive checks and model assessment.}
Model adequacy is assessed through posterior predictive checks in the sense of \citet{gelman1996posterior}. These are Bayesian diagnostics based on the posterior predictive distribution, not frequentist goodness-of-fit tests. For each posterior draw $\vartheta^{(m)}$, one generates a replicated data set by first drawing
\[
  A_i^{\mathrm{rep},(m)} \mid R_i = r, \vartheta^{(m)} \sim \Bernoulli\!\left(\pi_r^{(m)}\right),
\]
and then, for those replicated subjects with $A_i^{\mathrm{rep},(m)} = 1$,
\[
  Y_i^{\mathrm{rep},(m)} \mid \bigl(A_i^{\mathrm{rep},(m)} = 1, R_i = r, \vartheta^{(m)}\bigr)
  \sim F_r^{c,(m)}.
\]
The replicated combined outcome is finally reconstructed as $\widetilde{Y}_i^{\mathrm{rep},(e_0,m)} = e_0$ when $A_i^{\mathrm{rep},(m)} = 0$ and $\widetilde{Y}_i^{\mathrm{rep},(e_0,m)} = Y_i^{\mathrm{rep},(m)}$ otherwise. The observed data and the replicated data are then compared through discrepancies chosen to target the two components of the model.

For the atom part, the relevant question is whether the fitted Bernoulli model reproduces the observed armwise frequency of the atom. A natural discrepancy is therefore the maximum armwise absolute deviation between the observed atom rate and the fitted atom probability,
\[
  T_A(\widetilde{y},\vartheta)
  =
  \max_{r \in \{0,1\}}
  \left|
    \frac{1}{n_r}\sum_{i:R_i=r}\mathbbm{1}\{\widetilde{y}_i = e_0\} - \rho_r
  \right|.
\]
For the continuous non-atom part, the relevant question is whether the fitted mixture reproduces the shape of the conditional distribution among subjects with observed outcomes. The implementation therefore uses the maximum armwise Kolmogorov--Smirnov discrepancy between the empirical non-atom distribution and the fitted continuous mixture CDF,
\[
  T_C(y,\vartheta)
  =
  \max_{r \in \{0,1\}}
  \sup_{t \in \mathcal{C}}
  \left|
    \widehat{F}_{r,\mathrm{obs}}^c(t) - F_r^c(t \mid \vartheta)
  \right|.
\]
For bounded reported-score models, the same diagnostic is evaluated as a maximum armwise discrepancy between the empirical score CDF and the fitted discrete score CDF. For positive-support models the same comparison can be displayed on the log scale for graphical stability, but the target remains the arm-specific conditional non-atom law. These checks are supplemented naturally by graphical posterior predictive displays, such as grouped bar plots for the atom indicators and density, mass, or CDF overlays for the non-atom outcomes.

More generally, one may also examine scalar posterior predictive discrepancies based on arm-specific means, tail probabilities, quantiles, or other summaries chosen for the application. For any discrepancy $T(\cdot,\vartheta)$, the posterior predictive $p$-value is
\[
  p_{\mathrm{ppc}}
  =
  \Pr\!\left\{
    T(\widetilde{Y}^{\mathrm{rep}},\vartheta)
    \geq
    T(\widetilde{Y},\vartheta)
    \,\middle|\,
    \mathcal{D}_n
  \right\},
\]
estimated by the corresponding empirical proportion over posterior draws and replicated data sets. Such quantities are useful diagnostics for systematic lack of fit in either the atom model or the continuous conditional distribution, but they should be interpreted as Bayesian model-checking summaries rather than as classical significance tests.

\paragraph{Choosing the truncation level.}
Because the infinite stick-breaking mixture is implemented through a finite truncation, the number of retained mixture components $H$ should be viewed as an approximation-control parameter rather than as an estimand for a ``true'' finite number of mixture components. The inferential target is the posterior of the scientific functionals in Equation~(\ref{eq:bayes-estimands}), not the posterior of an exact component count. This distinction is important in flexible mixture models, because nearby kernels may split or merge without materially changing either the fitted non-atom distribution or the posterior of the scientific summaries.

For arm $r \in \{0,1\}$, the truncated stick-breaking construction implies
\[
  v_{rh} \mid \alpha_r \sim \Beta(1,\alpha_r),
  \qquad
  h = 1,\ldots,H-1,
\]
with weights
\[
  w_{r1} = v_{r1},
  \qquad
  w_{rh} = v_{rh}\prod_{\ell=1}^{h-1}(1-v_{r\ell}),
  \quad h = 2,\ldots,H-1,
\]
and
\[
  w_{rH} = \prod_{\ell=1}^{H-1}(1-v_{r\ell}).
\]
Thus $w_{rH}$ is the residual stick mass after the first $H-1$ breaks, and the implementation assigns this remaining mass to the final retained component. If one were to continue the stick-breaking construction by one additional break $V_{rH} \mid \alpha_r \sim \Beta(1,\alpha_r)$, then the mass lying strictly beyond the retained truncation point would be $w_{rH}(1-V_{rH})$. Since
\[
  \E(1-V_{rH} \mid \alpha_r) = \frac{\alpha_r}{1+\alpha_r},
\]
a natural truncation diagnostic is
\begin{equation}
  T_{rH}
  = w_{rH}\frac{\alpha_r}{1+\alpha_r},
  \label{eq:truncation-diagnostic}
\end{equation}
which is the posterior expected omitted stick mass beyond $H$, conditional on the currently retained residual stick. Small values of $T_{rH}$ indicate that little posterior mass remains beyond the truncation boundary. This is more informative than monitoring $w_{rH}$ alone, because the last retained component may itself be genuinely occupied by data.

We therefore choose $H$ by monitoring the posterior of $T_{rH}$ rather than by treating the number of occupied components as a scientific target. Let
\[
  n_r^{\mathrm{obs}}
  =
  \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbbm{1}(A_i = 1, R_i = r)
\]
denote the number of observed non-atom outcomes in arm $r$, and define the arm-specific tolerance
\[
  \eta_r = \min\!\left\{0.02,\frac{1}{n_r^{\mathrm{obs}}}\right\}.
\]
Operationally, we regard a truncation level as adequate when, for both treatment arms,
\begin{equation}
  Q_{0.95}(T_{rH} \mid \mathcal{D}_n) < \eta_r,
  \label{eq:truncation-rule}
\end{equation}
and the Markov chain Monte Carlo diagnostics are satisfactory, with no divergent transitions, $\widehat{R} \leq 1.01$, and adequate bulk and tail effective sample sizes. If Equation~(\ref{eq:truncation-rule}) fails in either arm, then $H$ is increased and the model is refit; the final analysis uses the smallest $H$ satisfying the criterion.

In the present implementation the ladder $H = 10 \rightarrow 20 \rightarrow 40$ is especially natural under the default prior $\alpha_r \sim \mathrm{Gamma}(2,1)$. Conditional on $\alpha_r$,
\[
  \E(w_{rH} \mid \alpha_r)
  =
  \left(\frac{\alpha_r}{1+\alpha_r}\right)^{H-1},
\]
so that
\[
  \E(T_{rH})
  =
  \E\!\left[
    \left(\frac{\alpha_r}{1+\alpha_r}\right)^H
  \right].
\]
Under $\mathrm{Gamma}(2,1)$, this prior mean omitted mass is approximately $0.033$ for $H=10$, $0.0039$ for $H=20$, and $1.6 \times 10^{-4}$ for $H=40$. Thus $H=10$ already provides a moderate truncation, $H=20$ drives the prior expected omitted mass below one-half of one percent, and $H=40$ makes prior truncation error negligible for practical purposes. Posterior predictive checks remain essential for overall model assessment, but they address model adequacy rather than truncation adequacy and therefore complement, rather than replace, the diagnostic in Equation~(\ref{eq:truncation-rule}).

\paragraph{Covariate extensions.}
The no-covariate treatment-stratified model is the primary development in this paper, but the same two-part architecture extends naturally when baseline covariates are present. A direct extension retains treatment stratification and replaces the arm-specific constants $\pi_r$ and $F_r^c$ by covariate-dependent quantities $\pi_r(x)$ and $F_r^c(\cdot \mid x)$. For example, one may write
\begin{align*}
  A_i \mid R_i = r, X_i = x
  &\sim \Bernoulli\!\bigl(\pi_r(x)\bigr),\\
  Y_i \mid (A_i = 1, R_i = r, X_i = x)
  &\sim F_r^c(\cdot \mid x),
\end{align*}
and then let the component-specific kernel means vary with $x$ within each treatment arm. A convenient dependent Dirichlet process construction is
\begin{align*}
  Y_i \mid (A_i = 1, R_i = r, X_i = x, z_i = h)
  &\sim K\!\bigl(\cdot \mid m_{rh}(x),\vartheta_{rh}\bigr),\\
  m_{rh}(\cdot)
  &\sim \mathcal{GP}\!\bigl(\mu_{0h}(\cdot), C_h(\cdot,\cdot)\bigr),
\end{align*}
so that the kernel locations vary smoothly with the covariates while the random mixing distribution remains treatment-arm specific. This yields a predictor-dependent mixture model in the spirit of dependent Dirichlet process constructions \citep{maceachern1999dependent,gelfand2005bayesian}; see also \citet{rasmussen2006gaussian} for Gaussian process priors. Such a formulation preserves the two-part interpretation while allowing treatment groups to differ not only in atom probability but also in how the entire non-atom distribution evolves over the covariates.

If a more modular regression component is preferred, the Gaussian-process mean functions can be replaced by Bayesian additive regression trees on the kernel means, thereby retaining a flexible regression surface while leaving the mixture structure unchanged \citep{chipman2010bart}. This is attractive when nonlinear covariate effects and interactions are important but a fully predictor-dependent random measure is unnecessary.

When a covariate is purely categorical, especially when it indexes substantively related groups, a hierarchical construction is often more appropriate than a fully continuous predictor-dependent process. For levels $\ell = 1,\ldots,L$ of a factor covariate, one may introduce within each treatment arm
\[
  G_r^\star \sim \mathrm{DP}(\gamma_r,H_0),
  \qquad
  G_{r\ell} \mid G_r^\star \sim \mathrm{DP}(\alpha_r,G_r^\star),
\]
so that
\[
  F_{r\ell}^c(B)
  =
  \int_{\Theta} K(B \mid \theta)\,G_{r\ell}(d\theta),
  \qquad
  B \subseteq \mathcal{C}.
\]
This is a hierarchical Dirichlet process mixture model \citep{teh2006hierarchical}; ANOVA-type dependent random-measure formulations provide a closely related alternative for factor effects \citep{deiorio2004anova}. If the factor levels correspond to different hospital sites, such a hierarchical construction is especially natural. Hospitals are expected to have related but not identical outcome distributions because of differences in case mix, clinical practice, or measurement processes. The site-specific random measures $G_{r\ell}$ therefore allow each hospital to retain its own atom probability model and its own non-atom distribution, while the shared parent measure $G_r^\star$ induces borrowing of strength by encouraging the hospitals within a treatment arm to reuse common mixture atoms. In this way, data-rich sites stabilize inference for data-sparse sites without forcing the site-specific distributions to be identical. These covariate-adjusted extensions are conceptually straightforward continuations of the no-covariate framework, but the basic two-group model already captures the main ideas needed for the present paper.

\section{Simulation study}\label{sec:simulation-study}

The simulation study had two purposes: to assess null calibration and to compare power with standard one-dimensional analyses when treatment acts through the atom, the positive part, or both. In every replicate, the two treatment groups had equal size, we worked on the atom-at-zero numerical representation, equivalently the coded variable $\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0} = c_0(\widetilde{Y}_i)$, and each subject contributed the numerical combined outcome $\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0} = A_iY_i$, where $A_i \mid R_i = r$ was Bernoulli with scenario-specific probability $\pi_r$ and $Y_i \mid (A_i = 1, R_i = r)$ followed the corresponding survivor distribution.

Table~\ref{tab:simulation} lists the data-generating mechanisms. The scenarios were chosen to isolate the practically important comparisons: a low-atom normal reference case (Scenario~1), a pure atom-probability effect (Scenario~2), an antagonistic case in which the atom and continuous components move in opposite directions while the mean of the coded combined outcome stays fixed (Scenario~3), a concordant joint improvement case (Scenario~4), a gamma shape-change case with survivor-mean shift (Scenario~5), and a rare-outlier contamination case that is deliberately favorable to rank-based methods (Scenario~6).

\input{build/tables/simulation-scenarios.tex}

For each scenario we considered effect levels $h \in \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$, with $h = 0$ as the null configuration, and per-group sample sizes $n \in \{50,100,150,200,300,400,500\}$. This yielded $6 \times 4 \times 7 = 168$ scenario-effect-sample-size cells, and each cell used 25,000 Monte Carlo repetitions. Power and empirical size were estimated as rejection proportions at the 5\% level, so the worst-case Monte Carlo standard error of an estimated rejection rate was approximately $0.0032$. We compared the parametric and semi-parametric likelihood-ratio tests with Welch's two-sample $t$-test and the Wilcoxon rank-sum test applied directly to the combined outcome; Appendix~\ref{app:supplementary-simulation-results} records the exact simulation protocol and implementation details needed to reproduce the study.

The results support the main motivation for the proposed tests, but they also show that no single procedure is uniformly best. Scenario~1 is the reference case for the parametric assumptions: the survivor distribution is normal, the atom probability is unchanged, and treatment shifts the survivor mean. At $h=3$ and $n=150$, the likelihood-ratio tests rejected 95.4\% of replicates, compared with 85.4\% for Wilcoxon and 69.4\% for the $t$-test. In Scenario~2, where treatment acts only through the atom probability, all four procedures detected the effect similarly. The one-dimensional tests were slightly ahead at $n=150$ (66.1\% for Wilcoxon versus 64.4\% for either likelihood-ratio test), and by $n=500$ all four rejection rates were about 99\%.

Scenario~3 is the clearest verification of the two-component idea. In that antagonistic setting, the coded combined-outcome mean is fixed at every effect level even though the atom probability and survivor mean both change. At $h=3$ and $n=150$, the likelihood-ratio tests reached about 91.5\% power, whereas the Wilcoxon and $t$-tests remained near their null rejection rates. By $n=500$, both likelihood-ratio procedures rejected every replicate, while the one-dimensional tests still had little signal. Scenario~4 is the least discriminating non-null scenario for comparing methods: when both components improve concordantly, the effect is already visible on the coded combined-outcome scale, and all four methods have similar power.

The non-normal scenarios illustrate the role of the survivor distribution. In Scenario~5, the survivor distribution changes shape and mean. The semi-parametric likelihood-ratio test is consistently the most powerful procedure, the parametric likelihood-ratio test is close but lower, the $t$-test is intermediate, and Wilcoxon has poor power; at $h=3$ and $n=150$, the corresponding rejection rates were 61.5\%, 56.2\%, 34.6\%, and 9.0\%. Scenario~6 deliberately favors a rank test through rare outlier contamination. Wilcoxon therefore dominates, while the likelihood-ratio tests outperform the $t$-test but are not competitive with Wilcoxon; at $h=3$ and $n=150$, Wilcoxon rejected 99.5\% of replicates, compared with 34.1\% for the parametric likelihood-ratio test, 30.6\% for the semi-parametric likelihood-ratio test, and 23.7\% for the $t$-test.

The null-calibration results are mostly reassuring but not perfectly uniform. Across the full simulation run, all method failure rates were zero. Most null rejection rates were close to the nominal 5\% level for all four methods. The important exception was the semi-parametric likelihood-ratio test in the rare-outlier null setting: its rejection rate was 12.6\% at $n=50$ and 6.6\% at $n=100$, before settling near nominal from $n \ge 150$. Thus the main conclusions are strongest for moderate sample sizes, while very small contaminated samples require caution for the semi-parametric procedure. The long supplementary tables document the full scenario-by-sample-size patterns underlying Figure~\ref{fig:powerCurves}.

\section{Clinical application: IST-3 stroke trial}\label{sec:application}

We apply the proposed methods to \ApplicationDatasetDescription{} \citep{IST3Lancet2012,IST3DataShare}. Acute ischemic stroke is a natural setting for atom-plus-continuous endpoints: treatment may affect both mortality and functional health, and death before follow-up is not a missing quality-of-life value but a clinically meaningful terminal state. Among participants alive at 6 months with observed EQ-VAS, the EuroQol visual analogue score is a patient-centered measure on a 0 to 100 scale, with larger values indicating better self-rated health.

The IST-3 data were downloaded from Edinburgh DataShare. The fixed-width data file \texttt{ist3.dat} and accompanying SAS syntax identify allocated treatment, death by 6 months, and 6-month EQ-VAS. The syntax labels \texttt{itt\_treat} as allocated treatment, \texttt{dead6mo} as death before 6 months, and \texttt{euroqol6} as the 6-month EuroQol health state. We verified the treatment coding against the text treatment field: \texttt{itt\_treat = 0} corresponds to rt-PA and \texttt{itt\_treat = 1} corresponds to placebo/control. We therefore set $R=1$ for rt-PA and $R=0$ for placebo/control. The DataShare record is publicly downloadable after the end of the embargo, but reuse is conditional on the IST-3 Data Use Agreement; the data citation is \citet{IST3DataShare}, DOI \ApplicationISTThreeDoi{}.

For the application endpoint, we code
\[
  Y =
  \begin{cases}
    -1, & \text{death by 6 months},\\
    \text{6-month EQ-VAS}, & \text{alive with observed EQ-VAS},
  \end{cases}
  \qquad
  A = \mathbbm{1}(Y \ne -1).
\]
Thus the atom is $-1$, representing death by 6 months, and the non-atom component is EQ-VAS for participants alive with an observed 6-month score. Alive participants with missing 6-month EQ-VAS were excluded from this analysis because the non-atom component was not observed; deaths were retained through the atom. This left \ApplicationN{} participants for analysis, including \ApplicationControlN{} placebo/control participants and \ApplicationTreatmentN{} rt-PA participants. Table~\ref{tab:ist3-descriptives} reports the analysis sample, death counts, EQ-VAS summaries among those alive with observed scores, and the combined endpoint mean.

The likelihood-ratio procedures test the joint null of no treatment effect on the death atom probability and no treatment effect on non-atom EQ-VAS. The parametric LRT statistic was \ApplicationLRTStatistic{} with p-value \ApplicationLRTP{}, and the semiparametric LRT statistic was \ApplicationSPLRTStatistic{} with p-value \ApplicationSPLRTP{}. Clinically, $\mu_\delta$ is the rt-PA minus control mean EQ-VAS difference among participants alive with observed scores; $\alpha_\delta$ is the rt-PA/control odds ratio for being alive with observed EQ-VAS rather than at the death atom; and $\Delta$ is the rt-PA minus control mean of the combined endpoint after assigning death the value $-1$. The joint rejection should therefore be interpreted as evidence that the observed two-component endpoint distribution differs between randomized arms, not as a principal-stratum causal effect.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

We have proposed parametric and semi-parametric likelihood-ratio tests for continuous outcomes with a clinically meaningful atom. The key step is to retain the atomized outcome used in practice while testing the joint null of no treatment effect on the atom probability and no treatment effect on the positive part. This yields one prespecified p-value together with interpretable component-wise effect estimates and confidence regions.

The simulations show why that distinction matters. The clearest gain appears when the atom and positive part move in opposite directions, because that is the setting in which a one-dimensional composite can cancel real component-level signal. When only the atom probability changes or when both components move concordantly, one-dimensional tests can be similarly powerful. The IST-3 application serves the same purpose in a clinically recognizable setting: the joint tests detect two-component structure that is only weakly visible to standard analyses of the raw combined outcome.

This observed-data focus should also guide interpretation. The parameter pair $(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)$ is not a survivor-average causal effect or a principal-stratum estimand; it describes the two components of the observed outcome distribution under randomization. The procedures are asymptotic, require positive numbers of observed outcomes in both groups, and the parametric version is only as credible as the model specified for $Y \mid A = 1$. The semi-parametric version relaxes that distributional assumption, but it still relies on regularity of the Bernoulli model and on feasible empirical-likelihood constraints.

For trials that require a single prespecified primary analysis, the framework offers one joint p-value together with clinically interpretable follow-up summaries for the binary and continuous components. That makes it attractive when investigators want to preserve the meaning of a worst-rank or atom-at-zero endpoint without collapsing the scientific question to a single distribution on the combined scale.

Software implementing both procedures, including the code used to generate the manuscript analyses, is available on GitHub \citep{TruncCompGitHub}.

\section{Supplementary simulation results}\label{app:supplementary-simulation-results}

This appendix collects the simulation figures and tables referenced in Section~\ref{sec:simulation-study}. It also records the exact simulation protocol implemented in \texttt{simulation-study/scripts/run-simulation-study.R} and \texttt{simulation-study/R/simulation-study.R}.

The simulation study used the full factorial design generated by the code defaults: six scenarios, four effect levels $h \in \{0,1,2,3\}$, and seven per-group sample sizes $n \in \{50,100,150,200,300,400,500\}$, for a total of $168$ cells. Each replicate contained exactly $n$ control observations and $n$ treatment observations. Within group $r \in \{0,1\}$, the indicator $A$ of whether the continuous outcome was observed was drawn as $A \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(\pi_r)$. Conditional on $A = 1$, the positive outcome was drawn from a scenario-specific distribution $F_{r,h}$; conditional on $A = 0$, the numerical atom value was recorded as $0$. Thus the analyzed endpoint was always the atom-at-zero numerical combined outcome $\widetilde{Y}^{\,0} = AY$, equivalently $c_0(\widetilde{Y})$, and no covariate adjustment was used.

The six scenario families were as follows. Scenario~1 used $\pi_0 = \pi_1 = 0.85$, $Y \mid A = 1, R = 0 \sim N(3, 1)$, and $Y \mid A = 1, R = 1 \sim N(3 + \delta_h, 1)$ with $\delta_h \in \{0, 0.20, 0.35, 0.50\}$. Scenario~2 used $\pi_0 = 0.45$, $\pi_1 \in \{0.45, 0.50, 0.55, 0.60\}$, and $Y \mid A = 1, R = 0 \sim Y \mid A = 1, R = 1 \sim N(3, 1)$. Scenario~3 used $\pi_0 = 0.50$, $\pi_1 \in \{0.50, 0.525, 0.55, 0.60\}$, $Y \mid A = 1, R = 0 \sim N(3, 1)$, and $Y \mid A = 1, R = 1 \sim N(1.5/\pi_1, 1)$, so that $E(\widetilde{Y}^{\,0} \mid R = 1) = E(\widetilde{Y}^{\,0} \mid R = 0) = 1.5$ at every effect level even though both components change. Scenario~4 used $\pi_0 = 0.50$, $\pi_1 \in \{0.50, 0.55, 0.60, 0.65\}$, $Y \mid A = 1, R = 0 \sim N(3, 1)$, and $Y \mid A = 1, R = 1 \sim N(3 + 0.15h, 1)$. Scenario~5 used $\pi_0 = \pi_1 = 0.65$, $Y \mid A = 1, R = 0 \sim \Gamma(9, 3/9)$, and $Y \mid A = 1, R = 1 \sim \Gamma(k_h, m_h/k_h)$ with $(k_h, m_h) \in \{(9, 3.0), (6, 3.2), (4, 3.4), (2.5, 3.6)\}$, where $\Gamma(k,\theta)$ denotes a gamma distribution with shape $k$ and scale $\theta$. Scenario~6 used $\pi_0 = \pi_1 = 0.80$ and $Y \mid A = 1, R = r \sim 0.95\,N(3 + \delta_h r, 0.35^2) + 0.05\,N(10 + \delta_h r, 0.5^2)$ with $\delta_h \in \{0, 0.15, 0.25, 0.35\}$.

For each cell, the driver called \texttt{set.seed(20260416 + cell\_id - 1)} before generating the 25,000 replicates, where \texttt{cell\_id} increases first over scenarios, then over effect levels, and then over sample sizes. Each replicate was analyzed by four methods applied to the combined outcome data frame produced by \texttt{simulateTruncatedData()}: \texttt{wilcox.test(Y \textasciitilde{} R, exact = FALSE)} for the Wilcoxon rank-sum test, \texttt{t.test(Y \textasciitilde{} R)} for Welch's two-sample $t$-test, \texttt{truncComp(Y \textasciitilde{} R, atom = 0, method = "LRT")} for the parametric likelihood-ratio test, and \texttt{truncComp(Y \textasciitilde{} R, atom = 0, method = "SPLRT")} for the semi-parametric likelihood-ratio test. In the package implementation, the parametric test combines a logistic likelihood-ratio comparison for $A$ with a Gaussian likelihood-ratio comparison for $Y$ among observations with $A = 1$, whereas the semi-parametric test combines the same logistic component with an empirical-likelihood test for the survivor mean difference. Both proposed procedures report a two-degree-of-freedom $\chi^2$-calibrated joint p-value.

Within each cell, rejection rates were computed as the proportion of successful replicates with p-value below $0.05$ for each method, and the Monte Carlo standard error was computed from that rejection proportion using the number of successful replicates for the given method. If a method call errored or returned a non-finite p-value, that replicate was counted as a failure for that method. The aggregated results object stores method-specific failure rates and runtimes, and the supplementary tables report the largest failure rate across the four methods in each cell. For the manuscript displays, Figure~\ref{fig:powerCurves} uses the strongest non-null effect level ($h = 3$) across all six scenarios; Figure~\ref{fig:powerEffectCurves} uses all four effect levels at $n = 150$ per group across all six scenarios; and Figure~\ref{fig:type1Curves} uses the null cells with $h = 0$ across all six scenarios. The six long supplementary tables list the complete power results for all $168$ cells together with the expected coded combined-outcome contrast $E(\widetilde{Y}^{\,0} \mid R = 1) - E(\widetilde{Y}^{\,0} \mid R = 0)$ used in the code-generated summaries.

\section{Supplementary description of the Delta-scale profile and projected intervals}\label{app:supplementary-delta-intervals}

This appendix records the exact unadjusted \texttt{TruncComp2} constructions underlying \texttt{DeltaProfileCI} and \texttt{DeltaProjectedCI}. These intervals are currently implemented only for the no-covariate parametric and semi-parametric models described in Section~\ref{sec:method}. To unify notation, define
\[
  \mathcal{W}_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)
  =
  \begin{cases}
    W_n^P(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta), & \text{for the parametric fit},\\[0.4em]
    \widetilde{W}_n^P(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta), & \text{for the semi-parametric fit}.
  \end{cases}
\]
Thus $\mathcal{W}_n$ always denotes the fitted two-parameter joint likelihood-ratio surface on the $(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)$ scale.

For the binary component, let
\[
  \ell_{A,n}(\beta_0,\beta_\delta)
  =
  \sum_{i = 1}^n
  \left\{
    \log\left(1 + e^{\beta_0 + \beta_\delta R_i}\right)
    - A_i(\beta_0 + \beta_\delta R_i)
  \right\}
\]
denote the negative Bernoulli log-likelihood. For fixed $\beta_\delta$, define the profiled intercept
\[
  \widehat{\beta}_0(\beta_\delta)
  \in
  \arg\min_{\beta_0 \in \mathbb{R}} \ell_{A,n}(\beta_0,\beta_\delta),
\]
and the corresponding fitted observation probabilities
\[
  \widehat{\pi}_r(\beta_\delta)
  =
  \operatorname{logit}^{-1}\!\left(\widehat{\beta}_0(\beta_\delta) + r\beta_\delta\right),
  \qquad r \in \{0,1\}.
\]
For the continuous component, let $\widehat{\mu}_0(\mu_\delta)$ denote the profiled control-group mean among the observed outcomes under the fixed mean-difference constraint $\mu_\delta$:
\[
  \widehat{\mu}_0(\mu_\delta)
  \in
  \begin{cases}
    \arg\max\limits_{\mu_0,\Psi} L_{Y,n}(\mu_0,\mu_\delta,\Psi), & \text{parametric},\\[0.8em]
    \arg\min\limits_{\mu_0 \in \mathbb{R}} \widetilde{W}_{Y,n}(\mu_0,\mu_\delta), & \text{semi-parametric},
  \end{cases}
\]
and define $\widehat{\mu}_1(\mu_\delta) = \widehat{\mu}_0(\mu_\delta) + \mu_\delta$. The profiled atom-at-zero numerical combined-outcome contrast induced by the pair $(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)$ is then
\begin{align}
  g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)
  &=
  \widehat{\pi}_1(\beta_\delta)\widehat{\mu}_1(\mu_\delta)
  -
  \widehat{\pi}_0(\beta_\delta)\widehat{\mu}_0(\mu_\delta).
  \label{eq:delta-map}
\end{align}
Equation~(\ref{eq:delta-map}) is the map that the software evaluates at every joint candidate point on the atom-at-zero numerical combined-outcome scale. In particular, the reported point estimate \texttt{Delta} is $\widehat{\Delta}_n := g_n(\widehat{\mu}_\delta,\widehat{\beta}_\delta)$.

Let
\[
  c_{1,\alpha} = \chi^2_1(1-\alpha),
  \qquad
  c_{2,\alpha} = \chi^2_2(1-\alpha)
\]
denote the one- and two-degree-of-freedom upper quantiles used for the profile and projected intervals on the atom-at-zero numerical combined-outcome scale $\Delta$, respectively.

\subsection*{Projected interval}

The projected interval is the image of the two-dimensional simultaneous confidence region under the map in Equation~(\ref{eq:delta-map}). More precisely, the package reports the endpoint pair
\[
  \mathrm{CI}^{\Delta,\mathrm{proj}}_{1-\alpha}
  =
  \left[
    L^{\mathrm{proj}}_{1-\alpha},
    U^{\mathrm{proj}}_{1-\alpha}
  \right],
\]
where
\begin{align}
  L^{\mathrm{proj}}_{1-\alpha}
  &=
  \inf
  \left\{
    g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)
    :
    \mathcal{W}_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta) \leq c_{2,\alpha}
  \right\},\label{eq:delta-proj-lower}\\
  U^{\mathrm{proj}}_{1-\alpha}
  &=
  \sup
  \left\{
    g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)
    :
    \mathcal{W}_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta) \leq c_{2,\alpha}
  \right\}.\label{eq:delta-proj-upper}
\end{align}
Thus the projected interval is the smallest closed interval containing all $\Delta$ values induced by joint parameter pairs inside the $(1-\alpha)100\%$ simultaneous region for $(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)$.

For the grid-based approximation used by default, choose a rectangular grid
\[
  \mathcal{G}_n
  =
  \left\{
    (\mu_\delta^{(a)},\beta_\delta^{(b)})
    :
    a = 1,\ldots,A,\;
    b = 1,\ldots,B
  \right\}.
\]
At each grid point define
\[
  W_{ab} = \mathcal{W}_n(\mu_\delta^{(a)},\beta_\delta^{(b)}),
  \qquad
  g_{ab} = g_n(\mu_\delta^{(a)},\beta_\delta^{(b)}).
\]
The grid estimator of the projected interval is
\[
  \widehat{\mathrm{CI}}^{\Delta,\mathrm{proj,grid}}_{1-\alpha}
  =
  \left[
    \min\{g_{ab} : W_{ab} \leq c_{2,\alpha}\},
    \max\{g_{ab} : W_{ab} \leq c_{2,\alpha}\}
  \right],
\]
provided that the accepted grid set is non-empty. In the software, the grid bounds are enlarged iteratively until the accepted set no longer touches the boundary of the evaluated box, or until a prespecified maximum number of expansions is reached.

For the optimization-based version, let $B_n \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ be the current rectangular search box on the $(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)$ scale. The exact endpoint problems are the constrained programs
\begin{align}
  L^{\mathrm{proj}}_{1-\alpha}(B_n)
  &=
  \inf_{(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta) \in B_n}
  \left\{
    g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)
    :
    \mathcal{W}_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta) \leq c_{2,\alpha}
  \right\},\label{eq:delta-proj-opt-lower}\\
  U^{\mathrm{proj}}_{1-\alpha}(B_n)
  &=
  \sup_{(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta) \in B_n}
  \left\{
    g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)
    :
    \mathcal{W}_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta) \leq c_{2,\alpha}
  \right\}.\label{eq:delta-proj-opt-upper}
\end{align}
The implementation solves these box-constrained problems numerically by replacing the hard inequality with a one-sided quadratic penalty. Writing $x_+ = \max(x,0)$ and letting $\kappa_n > 0$ denote the large data-scaled penalty constant used in the code, the exact objectives passed to the optimizer are
\begin{align}
  Q^{\mathrm{proj},L}_{n,\alpha}(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)
  &=
  g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)
  +
  \kappa_n\bigl(\mathcal{W}_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta) - c_{2,\alpha}\bigr)_+^2,\label{eq:delta-proj-penalty-lower}\\
  Q^{\mathrm{proj},U}_{n,\alpha}(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)
  &=
  -g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)
  +
  \kappa_n\bigl(\mathcal{W}_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta) - c_{2,\alpha}\bigr)_+^2.\label{eq:delta-proj-penalty-upper}
\end{align}
These expressions are obtained directly from Equations~(\ref{eq:delta-proj-opt-lower})--(\ref{eq:delta-proj-opt-upper}) by taking the lower-endpoint target $g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)$ or the upper-endpoint target $-g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)$ and then adding a quadratic penalty for positive excess above the simultaneous-region threshold. After numerical optimization of Equation~(\ref{eq:delta-proj-penalty-lower}) or~(\ref{eq:delta-proj-penalty-upper}) within $B_n$, the software moves along the line segment from the fitted center point to the optimizer proposal and performs binary search on that segment to recover the last feasible point satisfying $\mathcal{W}_n \leq c_{2,\alpha}$. If either endpoint lies too close to the box boundary, the box is expanded and the search is repeated.

\subsection*{Profile interval}

The $\Delta$-profile statistic is the one-dimensional profile of the same joint surface under the nonlinear constraint in Equation~(\ref{eq:delta-map}). For any target value $\Delta \in \mathbb{R}$ on the atom-at-zero numerical combined-outcome scale, define
\begin{equation}
  T_n(\Delta)
  =
  \inf
  \left\{
    \mathcal{W}_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)
    :
    g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta) = \Delta
  \right\}.
  \label{eq:delta-profile-stat}
\end{equation}
The corresponding profile interval is
\[
  \mathrm{CI}^{\Delta,\mathrm{prof}}_{1-\alpha}
  =
  \left\{
    \Delta \in \mathbb{R}
    :
    T_n(\Delta) \leq c_{1,\alpha}
  \right\}.
\]
The package reports this set through its lower and upper endpoints,
\[
  \mathrm{CI}^{\Delta,\mathrm{prof}}_{1-\alpha}
  =
  \left[
    L^{\mathrm{prof}}_{1-\alpha},
    U^{\mathrm{prof}}_{1-\alpha}
  \right],
\]
where
\[
  L^{\mathrm{prof}}_{1-\alpha}
  =
  \inf\{\Delta : T_n(\Delta) \leq c_{1,\alpha}\},
  \qquad
  U^{\mathrm{prof}}_{1-\alpha}
  =
  \sup\{\Delta : T_n(\Delta) \leq c_{1,\alpha}\}.
\]
Whenever the infimum in Equation~(\ref{eq:delta-profile-stat}) is attained on each non-empty fiber $\{(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta): g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta) = \Delta\}$, the accepted $\Delta$ values can equivalently be written as
\[
  \left\{
    g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)
    :
    \mathcal{W}_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta) \leq c_{1,\alpha}
  \right\},
\]
which explains the grid approximation used in the package. Specifically, on the same joint grid $\mathcal{G}_n$ defined above, the default grid estimator is
\[
  \widehat{\mathrm{CI}}^{\Delta,\mathrm{prof,grid}}_{1-\alpha}
  =
  \left[
    \min\{g_{ab} : W_{ab} \leq c_{1,\alpha}\},
    \max\{g_{ab} : W_{ab} \leq c_{1,\alpha}\}
  \right].
\]
Thus the profile and projected grid intervals are computed from the same evaluated joint surface and differ only in the threshold: $c_{1,\alpha}$ for the profile interval and $c_{2,\alpha}$ for the projected interval.

For the optimization-based profile interval, the inner profile problem in Equation~(\ref{eq:delta-profile-stat}) is reduced to a one-dimensional optimization over $\beta_\delta$.

In the parametric Gaussian case, write
\[
  N_+ = \sum_{i = 1}^n A_i,
  \qquad
  \overline{Y}_+
  =
  \frac{1}{N_+}\sum_{i : A_i = 1} Y_i,
  \qquad
  \overline{R}_+
  =
  \frac{1}{N_+}\sum_{i : A_i = 1} R_i.
\]
For fixed $\mu_\delta$, the constrained Gaussian fit among observed outcomes minimizes
\[
  S_n(\mu_0;\mu_\delta)
  =
  \sum_{i : A_i = 1}
  \left(Y_i - \mu_0 - \mu_\delta R_i\right)^2.
\]
Differentiating with respect to $\mu_0$ and setting the derivative to zero gives
\[
  \widehat{\mu}_0(\mu_\delta)
  =
  \overline{Y}_+ - \overline{R}_+\,\mu_\delta,
  \qquad
  \widehat{\mu}_1(\mu_\delta)
  =
  \overline{Y}_+ + (1-\overline{R}_+)\mu_\delta.
\]
Substituting these expressions into Equation~(\ref{eq:delta-map}) yields the exact affine representation on the atom-at-zero numerical combined-outcome scale
\begin{align}
  g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)
  &=
  \bigl(\widehat{\pi}_1(\beta_\delta) - \widehat{\pi}_0(\beta_\delta)\bigr)\overline{Y}_+\nonumber\\
  &\quad +
  \bigl\{\widehat{\pi}_1(\beta_\delta)(1-\overline{R}_+) + \widehat{\pi}_0(\beta_\delta)\overline{R}_+\bigr\}\mu_\delta.
  \label{eq:delta-parametric-affine}
\end{align}
Therefore, for fixed target $\Delta$ and fixed $\beta_\delta$, the unique compatible mean-difference value is
\begin{equation}
  \mu_\delta^{\star}(\Delta,\beta_\delta)
  =
  \frac{
    \Delta
    -
    \bigl(\widehat{\pi}_1(\beta_\delta) - \widehat{\pi}_0(\beta_\delta)\bigr)\overline{Y}_+
  }{
    \widehat{\pi}_1(\beta_\delta)(1-\overline{R}_+) + \widehat{\pi}_0(\beta_\delta)\overline{R}_+
  },
  \label{eq:delta-parametric-mudelta}
\end{equation}
Whenever the denominator is positive, Equation~(\ref{eq:delta-profile-stat}) reduces exactly to the one-dimensional problem
\begin{equation}
  T_n(\Delta)
  =
  \inf_{\beta_\delta \in \mathbb{R}}
  Q^{\mathrm{prof,par}}_n(\Delta,\beta_\delta),
  \qquad
  Q^{\mathrm{prof,par}}_n(\Delta,\beta_\delta)
  =
  \mathcal{W}_n\bigl(\mu_\delta^{\star}(\Delta,\beta_\delta),\beta_\delta\bigr).
  \label{eq:delta-profile-parametric-objective}
\end{equation}

In the semi-parametric case, the same reduction is available conceptually but not in closed form. For fixed $\mu_\delta$, let
\[
  \widehat{\mu}_0(\mu_\delta)
  \in
  \arg\min_{\mu_0 \in \mathbb{R}} \widetilde{W}_{Y,n}(\mu_0,\mu_\delta),
  \qquad
  \widehat{\mu}_1(\mu_\delta)
  =
  \widehat{\mu}_0(\mu_\delta) + \mu_\delta,
\]
so that Equation~(\ref{eq:delta-map}) defines a nonlinear map $(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta) \mapsto g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta)$. For fixed $(\Delta,\beta_\delta)$, the compatible mean-difference value $\mu_\delta^{\star}(\Delta,\beta_\delta)$ is any feasible solution of
\[
  g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta) = \Delta.
\]
Hence the exact profile objective is
\begin{equation}
  Q^{\mathrm{prof,sp}}_n(\Delta,\beta_\delta)
  =
  \begin{cases}
    \mathcal{W}_n\bigl(\mu_\delta^{\star}(\Delta,\beta_\delta),\beta_\delta\bigr),
    & \text{if a feasible solution exists},\\[0.6em]
    \infty,
    & \text{otherwise}.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:delta-profile-semiparametric-objective}
\end{equation}
The implementation constructs $\mu_\delta^{\star}(\Delta,\beta_\delta)$ by searching over the empirical-likelihood feasible range
\[
  \left[
    \min_{j \in \mathcal{I}_1} Y_j - \max_{i \in \mathcal{I}_0} Y_i,\;
    \max_{j \in \mathcal{I}_1} Y_j - \min_{i \in \mathcal{I}_0} Y_i
  \right].
\]
If the function $\mu_\delta \mapsto g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta) - \Delta$ changes sign over that interval, the code solves the equation by root-finding. Otherwise it minimizes the exact squared-mismatch objective
\begin{equation}
  M_n(\mu_\delta;\Delta,\beta_\delta)
  =
  \left\{g_n(\mu_\delta,\beta_\delta) - \Delta\right\}^2
  \label{eq:delta-profile-semiparametric-inner-objective}
\end{equation}
over the feasible interval and accepts the resulting candidate only if the remaining discrepancy is below numerical tolerance. The outer profile statistic is then
\begin{equation}
  T_n(\Delta)
  =
  \inf_{\beta_\delta \in \mathbb{R}}
  Q^{\mathrm{prof,sp}}_n(\Delta,\beta_\delta).
  \label{eq:delta-profile-semiparametric-outer}
\end{equation}

Once the profile function $\Delta \mapsto T_n(\Delta)$ has been defined, the lower and upper profile endpoints are the roots of $T_n(\Delta) = c_{1,\alpha}$ on the two sides of the fitted value $\widehat{\Delta}_n$. Numerically, the software brackets these roots by step-doubling away from $\widehat{\Delta}_n$ and then refines the bracket by bisection until the desired tolerance is reached.

\section{Supplementary derivation of the semi-parametric test statistic}\label{app:supplementary-derivation}

This appendix records the constrained empirical-likelihood calculation underlying Equation~(\ref{eq:nonparametric}). The grouped expression derived below is algebraically equivalent to the full-sample representation in Equation~(\ref{eq:nonparametric}) because $\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0} = 0$ whenever $A_i = 0$. Throughout, the treatment-group means among the observed outcomes are parameterized as $\mu_0$ for group $R = 0$ and $\mu_1 = \mu_0 + \mu_\delta$ for group $R = 1$.

More explicitly, the full-sample notation in Equation~(\ref{eq:nonparametric}) uses the factors $1-R_i$ and $R_i$ to select the control and treatment contributions, while the factors involving $A_i$ suppress subjects with $A_i = 0$. If $R_i = 0$ and $A_i = 1$, then $\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0} = Y_i$ and the control-arm summand reduces to $\log\{1 + \lambda_0(Y_i - \mu_0)\}$. If $R_i = 1$ and $A_i = 1$, then the treatment-arm summand reduces to $\log\{1 + \lambda_1(Y_i - \mu_0 - \mu_\delta)\}$. If $A_i = 0$, then $\widetilde{Y}_i^{\,0} = 0$ and both summands equal $\log(1) = 0$. Thus the full-sample display is exactly the same criterion as the grouped notation indexed by $\mathcal{I}_0$ and $\mathcal{I}_1$.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:empirical-likelihood-derivation}
Fix $(\mu_0,\mu_\delta) \in \mathbb{R}^2$ such that $\mu_0$ lies in the interior of the convex hull of $\{Y_i : i \in \mathcal{I}_0\}$ and $\mu_0 + \mu_\delta$ lies in the interior of the convex hull of $\{Y_j : j \in \mathcal{I}_1\}$. Define the normalized empirical likelihood ratio
\begin{align}
  R_{Y,n}^{\mathrm{E}}(\mu_0,\mu_\delta)
  = \sup_{\{p_i\},\{q_j\}}
  \left\{
    \prod_{i \in \mathcal{I}_0} |\mathcal{I}_0|p_i
    \prod_{j \in \mathcal{I}_1} |\mathcal{I}_1|q_j
  \right\}\label{eq:empiricalLik1}
\end{align}
subject to
\begin{alignat*}{3}
  p_i &\geq 0, &\quad \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_0} p_i &= 1, &\quad \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_0} p_i(Y_i - \mu_0) &= 0,\\
  q_j &\geq 0, &\quad \sum_{j \in \mathcal{I}_1} q_j &= 1, &\quad \sum_{j \in \mathcal{I}_1} q_j(Y_j - \mu_0 - \mu_\delta) &= 0.
\end{alignat*}
Then the maximizer in Equation~(\ref{eq:empiricalLik1}) is unique and is given by
\begin{align*}
  p_i = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{I}_0|\left(1 + \lambda_0(Y_i - \mu_0)\right)},
  \qquad
  q_j = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{I}_1|\left(1 + \lambda_1(Y_j - \mu_0 - \mu_\delta)\right)},
\end{align*}
where $(\lambda_0,\lambda_1)$ satisfy
\begin{align*}
  \frac{1}{|\mathcal{I}_0|}\sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_0}
  \frac{Y_i - \mu_0}{1 + \lambda_0(Y_i - \mu_0)} &= 0,\\
  \frac{1}{|\mathcal{I}_1|}\sum_{j \in \mathcal{I}_1}
  \frac{Y_j - \mu_0 - \mu_\delta}{1 + \lambda_1(Y_j - \mu_0 - \mu_\delta)} &= 0.
\end{align*}
Consequently,
\begin{align*}
  \log R_{Y,n}^{\mathrm{E}}(\mu_0,\mu_\delta)
  &= -\sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_0}\log\left(1 + \lambda_0(Y_i - \mu_0)\right)
     - \sum_{j \in \mathcal{I}_1}\log\left(1 + \lambda_1(Y_j - \mu_0 - \mu_\delta)\right),\\
  \widetilde{W}_{Y,n}(\mu_0,\mu_\delta)
  &= -2\log R_{Y,n}^{\mathrm{E}}(\mu_0,\mu_\delta)\\
  &= 2\left(
      \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_0}\log\left(1 + \lambda_0(Y_i - \mu_0)\right)
      + \sum_{j \in \mathcal{I}_1}\log\left(1 + \lambda_1(Y_j - \mu_0 - \mu_\delta)\right)
    \right),
\end{align*}
and the profiled statistic is
\begin{align*}
  \widetilde{W}_{Y,n}^P(\mu_\delta) = \inf_{\mu_0 \in \mathbb{R}} \widetilde{W}_{Y,n}(\mu_0,\mu_\delta).
\end{align*}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The objective in Equation~(\ref{eq:empiricalLik1}) is strictly concave on a convex feasible set. The convex-hull assumptions imply that the feasible set contains an interior point, hence the maximizer is unique and satisfies the first-order conditions. Introduce the Lagrangian
\begin{align}
  O(\{p_i\},\{q_j\})
  &= \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_0}\log(|\mathcal{I}_0|p_i)
   + \sum_{j \in \mathcal{I}_1}\log(|\mathcal{I}_1|q_j)\nonumber\\
  &\quad + \gamma_1\left(\sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_0} p_i - 1\right)
   + \gamma_2\left(\sum_{j \in \mathcal{I}_1} q_j - 1\right)\nonumber\\
  &\quad - |\mathcal{I}_0|\lambda_0\sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_0} p_i(Y_i - \mu_0)
   - |\mathcal{I}_1|\lambda_1\sum_{j \in \mathcal{I}_1} q_j(Y_j - \mu_0 - \mu_\delta).
\end{align}
Differentiation with respect to $p_i$ and $q_j$ yields
\begin{align}
  0 &= \frac{\partial O(\{p_i\},\{q_j\})}{\partial p_i}
  = \frac{1}{p_i} - |\mathcal{I}_0|\lambda_0(Y_i - \mu_0) + \gamma_1,\label{eq:lambda1}\\
  0 &= \frac{\partial O(\{p_i\},\{q_j\})}{\partial q_j}
  = \frac{1}{q_j} - |\mathcal{I}_1|\lambda_1(Y_j - \mu_0 - \mu_\delta) + \gamma_2.\label{eq:lambda2}
\end{align}
Multiplying Equation~(\ref{eq:lambda1}) by $p_i$ and summing over $i \in \mathcal{I}_0$, then using the constraints, gives
\begin{align*}
  0
  &= \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_0} p_i \frac{\partial O(\{p_i\},\{q_j\})}{\partial p_i}\\
  &= |\mathcal{I}_0| - |\mathcal{I}_0|\lambda_0\sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_0} p_i(Y_i - \mu_0) + \gamma_1\sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_0} p_i\\
  &= |\mathcal{I}_0| + \gamma_1.
\end{align*}
Hence $\gamma_1 = -|\mathcal{I}_0|$, and the same argument gives $\gamma_2 = -|\mathcal{I}_1|$. Substituting these identities into Equations~(\ref{eq:lambda1}) and~(\ref{eq:lambda2}) yields the stated optimizer weights.

The multiplier equations follow by imposing the moment constraints after substitution of the optimizer. Inserting the optimizer into Equation~(\ref{eq:empiricalLik1}) gives
\begin{align*}
  \log R_{Y,n}^{\mathrm{E}}(\mu_0,\mu_\delta)
  &= \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_0}\log(|\mathcal{I}_0|p_i)
   + \sum_{j \in \mathcal{I}_1}\log(|\mathcal{I}_1|q_j)\\
  &= -\sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_0}\log\left(1 + \lambda_0(Y_i - \mu_0)\right)
     - \sum_{j \in \mathcal{I}_1}\log\left(1 + \lambda_1(Y_j - \mu_0 - \mu_\delta)\right).
\end{align*}
The displayed expression for $\widetilde{W}_{Y,n}(\mu_0,\mu_\delta)$ is therefore immediate from the definition $\widetilde{W}_{Y,n}(\mu_0,\mu_\delta) = -2\log R_{Y,n}^{\mathrm{E}}(\mu_0,\mu_\delta)$, and the profiled statistic is obtained by minimizing over the nuisance mean $\mu_0$.
\end{proof}
